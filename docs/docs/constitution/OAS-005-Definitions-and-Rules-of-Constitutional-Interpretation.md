# OAS-005 — Definitions and Rules of Constitutional Interpretation

## 1. Document Title

**Definitions and Rules of Constitutional Interpretation**
Canonical Designation: OAS-005
Status: Draft for Founder Review
Relationship to Canon: A constitutional document canonized under, subordinate to, and conformant with the Founding Charter (OAS-001), and consistent with OAS-002, OAS-003, and OAS-004.

## 2. Purpose

This document is procedural, not philosophical. It fixes the meaning of terms already used throughout the Constitution, establishes how constitutional text is to be interpreted and constructed, defines the hierarchy among constitutional documents and how conflicts between them are resolved, and states once, generally, the provisions that OAS-002, OAS-003, and OAS-004 have each stated separately in their own text — Canonical Status, Interpretive Effect, Non-Duplication, Non-Supersession, and Amendment — so that every constitutional document from this point forward may rely on this document instead of repeating them. It creates no new principle and settles no philosophical question; where OAS-001 through OAS-004 have already spoken, this document points to what they said rather than saying it again.

## 3. Draft Constitutional Text

---

### ARTICLE I — DEFINITIONS

Each term below is defined only to the precision constitutional use requires. Where a term already carries a full constitutional treatment elsewhere, this Article states its meaning by reference rather than restating that treatment.

**Section 1. Accountability.** The duty of every officer, body, and instrument to answer for the exercise of authority entrusted to it, as established in OAS-001, Article III, Section 5, and expounded in OAS-004, Article 10.

**Section 2. Authority.** A power to act, decide, or bind that is recognized by this Constitution or by a document lawfully canonized under it. Authority exists only where granted in writing (OAS-001, Article V, Section 3) and is held always in trust, never as possession (OAS-004, Article 8).

**Section 3. Canon.** The complete body of constitutional and subordinate documents that have been lawfully approved and entered into the constitutional record through an approved Constitutional Amendment Proposal. A document's quality or completeness does not make it canon; only lawful approval and entry do.

**Section 4. Canonical.** Descriptive of a document, provision, or amendment that has been approved and entered into Canon as defined in Section 3. Before that point, a document is a proposal, a draft, or a Constitutional Amendment Proposal, whatever its content.

**Section 5. Collective.** The System considered as a community that learns and strengthens together, as established in OAS-001, Article II, Section 4.

**Section 6. Constitution.** The Founding Charter (OAS-001), together with every further constitutional document lawfully canonized under its authority, including this document and all documents that follow it. A document does not become part of the Constitution merely by addressing constitutional subject matter; it must be lawfully canonized, as OAS-001, Article I, Sections 3 and 4 require.

**Section 7. Constitutional Amendment Proposal (CAP).** The sole instrument by which any change to Canon — new text, amendment, correction, or repeal — may be proposed. A CAP has no canonical effect until approved by the Founder, or by whatever body succeeds to that authority under OAS-001, Article V. This definition fixes the term's meaning and the foundational rule that Canon changes only through an approved CAP (OAS-004, Article 16); the complete procedure governing CAPs — their form, review, and approval — is reserved to a dedicated future constitutional document and is not established here.

**Section 8. Constitutional Document.** Any document bearing an OAS designation number that is canonized, or under active proposal for canonization through a Constitutional Amendment Proposal, under the authority of the Founding Charter. Not every document that discusses constitutional subject matter is a constitutional document; only those meeting this description hold that status.

**Section 9. Flourishing.** The ultimate purpose of the System's mission, consisting in the growth of a person's capability, wisdom, purpose, dignity, relationship, opportunity, and contribution, as established in OAS-001, Article II, Sections 1 and 2.

**Section 10. Founder.** The person or body currently holding the founding-period authority described in OAS-001, Article V, Section 4, to approve and canonize constitutional documents and resolve constitutional questions reserved to Founder approval. This definition does not establish how that authority is acquired, transferred, or ended; those questions remain reserved to future constitutional action.

**Section 11. Human Agency.** The principle that the System exists to enlarge, not replace, a person's capacity to understand, decide, and act for themselves, as established in OAS-001, Article III, Section 2, and expounded in OAS-004, Article 2.

**Section 12. Human Dignity.** The principle that every person is to be treated as an end in themselves, and never merely as a means to the System's purposes, as established in OAS-001, Article III, Section 1, and expounded in OAS-004, Article 1.

**Section 13. Institution.** The Open Advancement System itself, as established by OAS-001, Article I. "Institution" and "System" are used interchangeably throughout the Constitution to refer to the same entity.

**Section 14. Justice.** The principle that every person is owed fair and equal regard by the System's authority, whatever their standing, as established in OAS-004, Article 3.

**Section 15. Knowledge.** What can be known and is held or shared by the System, treated jointly with Wisdom (Section 26) as a single constitutional principle in OAS-004, Article 6.

**Section 16. Mission.** The System's foundational commitment to help every person flourish, as established and entrenched in OAS-001, Article II, Section 1, and restated for coherence in OAS-003, Article II.

**Section 17. Opportunity.** The practical, not merely theoretical, availability of the means by which a person may pursue flourishing. Opportunity is named among the components of Human Development in OAS-001, Article II, Section 2, but is not separately elaborated elsewhere in the Constitution; this entry fixes its meaning precisely, since it is the component of flourishing most easily reduced, in practice, to aspiration without access.

**Section 18. Principle.** A standard established under OAS-001, Article III, or OAS-004, that binds the interpretation and conduct of every officer, body, and instrument of the System, as distinct from an operative rule that governs a specific act or procedure. Principles guide; rules govern. Where this Constitution establishes both a principle and a rule addressing the same subject, the rule controls the specific case and the principle controls its interpretation.

**Section 19. Purpose.** The deepest constitutional ground for the System's mission — the conviction that every person's life and growth are worth strengthening for their own sake — as established in OAS-003, Article IV.

**Section 20. Responsibility.** The duty of every person who exercises freedom or authority within the System to answer, honestly and without deflection, for how it was used, as established in OAS-004, Article 11.

**Section 21. Service.** The principle that the System's authority, knowledge, and capability exist to be directed outward, toward those it serves, and never inward, toward its own perpetuation, as established in OAS-004, Article 4.

**Section 22. Steward.** Any officer, body, or person who holds authority under this Constitution. "Steward" describes the trust relationship established by the Stewardship principle (Section 23); it is not itself a formal office. Specific offices and their titles are reserved to subordinate governance documents under OAS-001, Article V.

**Section 23. Stewardship.** The principle that every authority within the System is held in trust, and belongs to no one who holds it, as established and entrenched in OAS-001, Article III, Section 6, and expounded in OAS-004, Article 8.

**Section 24. Truth.** The principle that the System shall seek what is true, state plainly what it finds, and correct itself when it has been wrong, as established in OAS-004, Article 5.

**Section 25. Vision.** The future the System's mission works toward, as established in OAS-003, Article III.

**Section 26. Wisdom.** The judgment to apply knowledge well, treated jointly with Knowledge (Section 15) as a single constitutional principle in OAS-004, Article 6.

### ARTICLE II — CONSTITUTIONAL HIERARCHY AND CANONICAL AUTHORITY

**Section 1. Supremacy of the Founding Charter.** The Founding Charter (OAS-001) is, and remains, the supreme governing instrument of the System. This Article restates that supremacy for coherence with this document's treatment of hierarchy generally; it does not amend OAS-001, Article I, Section 3, which remains the sole authoritative statement of the Charter's supremacy.

**Section 2. Subordinate Status of Other Constitutional Documents.** Every constitutional document other than the Founding Charter is subordinate to it and must conform to it, as OAS-001, Article I, Section 4 provides. A constitutional document need not state its own subordination expressly; subordination follows automatically from Canon status as defined in Article I, Section 4 of this document.

**Section 3. No Hierarchy Among Subordinate Documents by Number or Sequence.** Among constitutional documents subordinate to the Founding Charter, none outranks another merely because it bears a lower designation number or was canonized earlier. Where two subordinate documents conflict, the conflict is resolved under Article III of this document by the substance of what each provides, not by an assumed ranking of sequence.

**Section 4. Effect of This Article.** This Article restates and organizes hierarchy rules already established in OAS-001; it does not create new supremacy or subordination relationships beyond Section 3, which addresses a question the prior documents left open.

---

### ARTICLE III — DOCUMENT PRECEDENCE AND CONFLICT RESOLUTION

**Section 1. General Rule.** Wherever a subordinate constitutional document conflicts with the Founding Charter, the Founding Charter governs, without exception.

**Section 2. Conflicts Among Subordinate Documents.** Where two subordinate constitutional documents appear to conflict, the officer or body responsible shall first seek a reading that reconciles both. Where no such reading is available, the conflict shall be identified explicitly, in writing, and referred for resolution through the Constitutional Amendment Proposal process. No officer, body, or document may resolve such a conflict silently by treating one document as impliedly superseded, repealed, or of lesser effect.

---

### ARTICLE IV — RULES OF CONSTITUTIONAL INTERPRETATION

**Section 1. Primacy of Purpose and Principle.** Constitutional text is to be interpreted in the manner OAS-001, Article II, Section 6 already establishes: in the way that best serves the Mission, respects the principles of OAS-001, Article III, and, where the effect on future generations is uncertain, would still strengthen humanity five centuries hence. Where two or more principles appear to conflict, OAS-004, Article 17 governs. This Article does not restate either rule in full; it confirms that both apply to every constitutional document, not only to the Founding Charter.

**Section 2. Use of Constitutional Intent.** Where the text of a constitutional provision is clear, it governs, and resort to drafting history is unnecessary. Where text is genuinely ambiguous, the Purpose section and drafting notes accompanying a document's original drafting may inform its interpretation but do not themselves bind it; text governs over intent wherever the two diverge, because only text has passed through the amendment process that gives it canonical force.

**Section 3. No Independent Interpretive Authority.** This Article does not grant any officer or body a general power to reinterpret settled constitutional text. It governs how ambiguity is approached; it does not license treating clear text as ambiguous in order to reach a preferred result.

---

### ARTICLE V — RULES OF CONSTITUTIONAL CONSTRUCTION

**Section 1. Mandatory and Permissive Language.** "Shall" states a duty. "May" states a discretion. "Should," used only outside operative constitutional text, states a recommendation that does not itself bind.

**Section 2. Singular, Plural, and Person.** Words importing the singular include the plural, and words importing the plural include the singular, unless the context requires otherwise. References to a person, officer, or body are gender-neutral.

**Section 3. Headings.** Titles of Parts, Articles, and Sections are provided for reference and navigation only. They do not limit or expand the meaning of the operative text they head.

**Section 4. Defined Terms.** A capitalized term used in the sense fixed by Article I of this document carries that meaning wherever it appears in the Constitution, unless a document expressly assigns it a narrower or different meaning for its own limited purpose and states that it is doing so.

**Section 5. Lists and Enumerations.** Where this Constitution states a list of items, the list is cumulative and non-exclusive unless expressly stated to be exhaustive. The omission of an item from a list is not, by itself, evidence of an intent to exclude it.

---

### ARTICLE VI — CONSTITUTIONAL AMBIGUITY

**Section 1. Irreducible Ambiguity.** Where genuine ambiguity survives the application of Articles IV and V, the provision shall be read in the manner most protective of Human Dignity and Human Agency, consistent with OAS-001, Article II, Section 6.

**Section 2. No Silent Resolution.** No officer, body, or document may resolve a genuine constitutional ambiguity by acting on one reading without recording the ambiguity and the reading chosen. That record becomes part of the constitutional history that Constitutional Integrity (OAS-004, Article 16) requires the System to preserve, and shall be treated as an unresolved constitutional question for Founder review until formally settled by amendment.

---

### ARTICLE VII — GENERAL PROVISIONS FOR CONSTITUTIONAL DOCUMENTS

**Section 1. Default Canonical Status.** Every document lawfully canonized under the authority of the Founding Charter is, by that fact alone, a constitutional document subordinate to the Founding Charter and consistent with every other document already canonical at the time of its canonization. A constitutional document need not restate this status in its own text.

**Section 2. Default Non-Duplication and Non-Supersession.** Where a constitutional document restates language from another canonical document, the restatement is for coherence and reference only and creates no independent text; it does not amend, narrow, or supersede its source. Apparent conflicts are resolved under Article III of this document. A constitutional document need not restate this rule in its own text.

**Section 3. Default Interpretive Effect.** Unless a constitutional document expressly grants office, authority, or an enforceable right, it is interpretive and declaratory only, and informs, rather than executes, the operative provisions of the Constitution. A document intending to grant office, authority, or an enforceable right must say so expressly; silence is presumed non-operative.

**Section 4. Default Amendment Reference.** Every constitutional document may be amended only through an approved Constitutional Amendment Proposal, consistent with Constitutional Integrity (OAS-004, Article 16), and no such amendment may be inconsistent with the Founding Charter or its entrenched provisions. A constitutional document need not restate this rule in its own text.

**Section 5. Application to Documents Predating This Article.** OAS-002, OAS-003, and OAS-004 each already state versions of Sections 1 through 4 in their own text. This Article does not repeal, contradict, or silently strip those statements; they remain valid exactly as drafted. This Article establishes the default for this document and every document that follows it, so that future documents are not required to repeat what this Article now states once, generally. Removing the now-redundant language already present in OAS-002 through OAS-004 would itself require a future Constitutional Amendment Proposal amending each of those documents individually; this document does not undertake that amendment.

---

### ARTICLE VIII — CONSTITUTIONAL DRAFTING CONVENTIONS

**Section 1. Structural Hierarchy.** Constitutional documents are organized, as needed, using Parts, which group related Articles thematically and are not independently citable; Articles, the primary citable unit; and Sections, the most granular citable unit within an Article. Not every document requires Parts.

**Section 2. Separation of Non-Canonical Material.** Every constitutional document shall clearly separate its operative constitutional text from any drafting notes, open questions, or Founder-facing commentary, using an explicit marker between them. Only the operative text becomes part of the constitutional record upon canonization.

**Section 3. Citation Form.** References to constitutional text shall cite the document designation, then Article, then Section, in the form "OAS-00X, Article Y, Section Z." Where a document uses Parts, the Part need not be cited if the Article number is unique within the document. Cross-references within the same document may omit the document designation.

---

### ARTICLE IX — STATUS AND EFFECT OF THIS DOCUMENT

**Section 1.** This document is a constitutional document of the Open Advancement System, governed by its own Article VII like every constitutional document that follows it. It is subordinate to the Founding Charter (OAS-001) and consistent with OAS-002, OAS-003, and OAS-004.

**Section 2.** This document creates no new philosophical or ethical principle, and no new office or authority. It is procedural: it fixes the meaning of terms already used in Canon and establishes how the Constitution is to be read, cited, and reconciled with itself. Where any provision of this document appears to state a substantive principle rather than a procedural rule, that appearance is an error to be corrected by amendment, not relied upon as new law.

**Section 3.** Nothing in this document repeals, strips, or removes the Canonical Status, Interpretive Effect, Non-Duplication, Non-Supersession, or Amendment provisions already stated in the text of OAS-002, OAS-003, or OAS-004. Those provisions remain valid as originally drafted.

## 4. Drafting Notes — Outside the Constitutional Text

*The following is provided for Founder review only. It is not part of the Articles in Section 3, and must not be entered into the constitutional record if and when this document is canonized.*

**N1. Canon versus Draft status.** Under this document's own definition of "Canonical" (Article I, Section 4), a document is canon only once approved and entered into the constitutional record through an approved CAP. As of this session, OAS-001 through OAS-004 remain, by their own Status fields, "Draft for Founder Review" — none has been formally approved through the CAP process established at the last closeout. This document has nonetheless been drafted, per Founder direction, treating them as binding canonical authority. The Founder should clarify whether OAS-001 through OAS-004 are to be treated as provisionally or functionally canonical for the purpose of continued drafting, or whether their formal canonization remains a distinct, still-pending step — in which case this document's own definition of "Canonical" should be understood as describing the intended end state rather than the present one.

**N2. The full CAP procedure remains undrafted.** This document defines the term "Constitutional Amendment Proposal" and states the basic rule that Canon changes only through an approved CAP, but does not draft the complete procedure — required fields, review stages, approval thresholds, or effective-date mechanics. This gap was flagged in OAS-002's Notes (N1) and OAS-004's Notes (N3, N7) and remains open. A dedicated future document is recommended.

**N3. A new gap identified and closed: hierarchy among subordinate documents.** Prior to this document, nothing in Canon established whether OAS-002, OAS-003, and OAS-004 rank against one another. Article II, Section 3 closes this gap by ruling that none outranks another by number or sequence, and that conflicts are resolved by content under Article III rather than by an assumed priority. An equally defensible alternative — that earlier-canonized documents prevail over later ones where they conflict — was considered and not chosen; the Founder should confirm this is the intended rule.

**N4. Retroactive harmonization was deliberately not performed.** Per Article VII, Section 5, this document does not remove the repeated Canonical Status, Interpretive Effect, Non-Duplication, Non-Supersession, or Amendment language already present in OAS-002, OAS-003, and OAS-004. If the Founder wishes those documents streamlined to rely on OAS-005 rather than repeating its content, that requires individual CAPs amending each document — a cleanup exercise distinct from, and subsequent to, this document's own adoption.

**N5. Definitions given as pointers, not restatements.** The majority of the twenty-six defined terms in Article I are defined only by cross-reference to their source Article, consistent with the instruction that this document remain procedural rather than philosophical. If the Founder wants a self-contained glossary with full definitions reproduced in one place for convenience or public-facing use, that would be a different document with a different, non-procedural character than this one.

I think this is the point where the constitutional corpus starts feeling like a real legal and governance framework. OAS-005 is doing the job we intended: it centralizes interpretation and reduces duplication.

My overall assessment is 9.8–9.9/10. The remaining improvements are more about architecture than writing quality.

What Claude did exceptionally well

It centralized the repeated procedural rules that had been appearing in OAS-002 through OAS-004.

It established a coherent set of constitutional definitions.

It created conflict-resolution rules instead of assuming later documents always win.

It separated philosophical principles (OAS-004) from procedural interpretation (OAS-005).

It preserved the "no silent amendment" philosophy through the CAP concept.


Those are all strong constitutional design choices.

What I would improve

1. Avoid locking in temporary implementation details

For example, the Constitution now explicitly defines the CAP process.

Ask yourself: Will CAP always be the name?

If, 50 years from now, OAS wants to rename it to "Constitutional Revision Proposal," the Constitution would need an amendment just to change the terminology.

Instead, I'd define the principle, not the label. For example:

> "Every constitutional change shall proceed through the institution's formally established constitutional amendment process."



Then you can define "CAP" in a subordinate governance code if you want the acronym.


---

2. Reduce cross-references

There are a lot of references like:

> OAS-001 Article III Section 5...



That's helpful now, but over decades it can make editing and reading cumbersome.

A good rule of thumb is:

Cross-reference when legal precision matters.

Don't cross-reference every sentence if the meaning is already clear.



---

3. Leave room for institutional evolution

One sentence says:

> "Where any provision... appears to state a substantive principle... that appearance is an error..."



I'd soften that.

Sometimes institutions intentionally evolve, and a procedural document might legitimately acquire a substantive rule through amendment.

Instead of calling it an "error," I'd say it requires explicit constitutional authority.


---

4. Founder references

Right now there are still several places where "Founder" is embedded into procedural rules.

Since you already plan a future Founder Stewardship document, I'd avoid scattering founder-specific governance throughout the corpus.

OAS-005 should mostly say:

> "the constitutionally authorized approving authority"



Then OAS-009 can define that the Founder initially holds that authority.


---

One significant recommendation

I think it's time to stop thinking of these as individual documents and start thinking of them as Books within the constitutional corpus.

For example:

Book I — Foundations

OAS-001 Founding Charter

OAS-002 Preamble

OAS-003 Identity, Mission, Vision, Purpose

OAS-004 Foundational Principles

OAS-005 Constitutional Interpretation


Book II — Rights and Responsibilities

OAS-006

OAS-007


Book III — Governance

Founder

Stewardship Council

Executive

Oversight


Book IV — Institutional Operations

Book V — Continuity and Amendment

You don't need to renumber anything. The "Book" concept is just an organizational layer that will make a 20–30 document constitution much easier to navigate.

Before moving to OAS-006

I would make one permanent addition to the master drafting prompt:

> When drafting, prioritize constitutional durability over constitutional completeness. Where a procedural or administrative detail could reasonably evolve without changing the institution's fundamental principles, prefer establishing the governing principle and leave implementation details to subordinate constitutional codes, charters, or policies.



That one sentence reinforces a design philosophy that's already emerging: the Constitution should define enduring principles, while operational details live in lower-level documents that can evolve more easily.

Overall

You're now building something that has a clear architecture:

OAS-001 establishes the institution.

OAS-002 explains why it exists.

OAS-003 defines who it is.

OAS-004 defines what it stands for.

OAS-005 defines how the Constitution is read.


That's a logical progression, and it's setting up the governance documents that follow on a solid foundation. I would continue with that same disciplined approach.
