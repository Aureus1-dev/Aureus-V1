# OAS-007 — Duties, Responsibilities, and Civic Obligations

## 1. Document Title

**Duties, Responsibilities, and Civic Obligations**
Canonical Designation: OAS-007
Status: Draft for Founder Review
Relationship to Canon: A constitutional document canonized under, subordinate to, and conformant with the Founding Charter (OAS-001), and consistent with OAS-002 through OAS-006. Governed by the default rules of OAS-005, Article VII, except where this document expressly provides otherwise.

## 2. Purpose

This document establishes the enduring duties, responsibilities, and civic obligations that correspond to the constitutional rights established in OAS-006. It is written under one governing constraint, stated fully in Article 21: no duty established here may condition, offset, or diminish any right established in OAS-006. Duties are owed to the constitutional order, to the Mission, and to other persons; they are not a price paid for rights, which this Constitution grants without regard to conduct. Where a duty restates an obligation already established in OAS-001, this document translates it, without amendment, from an institutional or official rule into a stated civic obligation; where a duty is established here for the first time, it is identified as such. This document is expressly operative, pursuant to OAS-005, Article VII, Section 3.

## 3. Draft Constitutional Text

---

### PART ONE — DUTIES THAT MIRROR RIGHTS

*The duties of this Part are owed by every person interacting with the System, and correspond to the universal rights and procedural protections of OAS-006, Parts One and Two.*

#### Article 1. Duty of Mutual Dignity

**Section 1.** Every person owes to every other person the same treatment as an end in themselves that the System owes under OAS-006, Article 1. This duty is established here for the first time as a person-to-person obligation; OAS-006, Article 1 and OAS-001, Article III, Section 1 bind the System's own conduct, not the conduct of persons toward one another.

**Section 2.** This duty creates no new enforcement mechanism of its own. Where one person's dignity is violated by another rather than by the System, the injured person's remedy runs through whatever governance or dispute mechanism a future subordinate document establishes; this Article states the obligation and does not itself supply that mechanism.

#### Article 2. Duty to Respect the Agency of Others

**Section 1.** Every person owes to every other person the same respect for independent understanding, decision, and action that Human Agency (OAS-006, Article 2) guarantees against the System.

**Section 2.** This duty does not require deference to another's choices where those choices would themselves violate this Constitution. It requires only that persons not treat one another as instruments within the System's shared spaces.

#### Article 3. Duty of Fair Dealing

**Section 1.** Every person owes the System, and every other person, the same fairness and consistency that the Right to Fair Treatment (OAS-006, Article 3) requires of the System.

**Section 2.** This duty does not require identical outcomes in every interaction; it requires the standard the System itself must meet — no distinction drawn on the basis of standing, station, or convenience.

#### Article 4. Duty of Honesty

**Section 1.** Every person owes the System an honest account of matters material to the System's decisions concerning them, corresponding to the Right to Truthful Treatment the System owes in return (OAS-006, Article 4).

**Section 2.** This duty is reciprocal, not conditional: a person's own honesty is not a precondition for the System's duty of truthfulness toward them, which OAS-006, Article 4 states without qualification. It exists because a constitutional order that requires truth of itself but tolerates deception from everyone else cannot sustain the trust every other principle in this Constitution depends on.

#### Article 5. Duty to Respect the Privacy of Others

**Section 1.** Every person owes to every other person the same respect for control over personal information that the Right to Privacy (OAS-006, Article 5) guarantees against the System.

**Section 2.** As with Article 1, this duty creates an obligation without an accompanying enforcement mechanism; its practical protection is reserved to future subordinate documents.

#### Article 6. Duty of Good Faith in Constitutional Process

**Section 1.** Every person invoking the procedural protections of OAS-006, Part Two — Notice, Explanation, Human Review, and Petition — owes the System engagement in good faith: an accurate representation of the matter at issue, and use of these mechanisms for their intended purpose rather than for harassment or delay.

**Section 2.** This duty protects the integrity of the mechanisms Part Two of OAS-006 establishes; a right that can be weaponized in bad faith becomes, in practice, unavailable to those who need it in good faith. This duty does not authorize the System to deny Notice, Explanation, Human Review, or Petition on a unilateral finding of bad faith; any such finding is itself a decision subject to the same procedural protections it concerns (see Article 21).

---

### PART TWO — DUTIES THAT PROTECT THE CONSTITUTIONAL ORDER

#### Article 7. Duty to Contribute to Institutional Learning

**Section 1.** Every person who becomes aware, through their own experience with the System, of an error, a risk, or a lesson that would strengthen the mission is encouraged to make it known through the means the constitutional order provides. Every Steward bears this as a duty, not merely an encouragement (see Article 15).

**Section 2.** This Article distinguishes an encouragement, extended to every person, from a duty, owed specifically by Stewards. The distinction reflects that Institutional Learning (OAS-001, Article III, Section 8) is an institutional duty the System owes itself, not a legal obligation properly imposed on persons who have not accepted the responsibilities of office.

#### Article 8. Duty to Preserve Space for Disagreement

**Section 1.** Every Steward owes a duty not to suppress, penalize, or discourage good-faith criticism or disagreement expressed through constitutional means. Raising such criticism fulfills, rather than breaches, a person's civic relationship to the System.

**Section 2.** This Article gives operative effect to Truth (OAS-004, Article 5) and is reinforced by the Right to Be Free from Retaliation (OAS-006, Article 10). Together they establish that good-faith dissent is a constitutionally protected form of participation, not a tolerated exception to loyalty.

#### Article 9. Duty to Uphold Stewardship

**Section 1.** Every person who holds any authority within the System, however minor or temporary, owes the same duty of trust that Stewardship (OAS-001, Article III, Section 6; OAS-004, Article 8) imposes on the System's officers: to exercise that authority for the benefit of those it touches, and never as personal possession.

**Section 2.** This duty applies by degree, not by office alone. A person entrusted with a single task owes it as much as an officer entrusted with an institution, though the consequences of breach differ according to Article V of the Founding Charter, which governs formal office.

#### Article 10. Duty to Support Constitutional Integrity

**Section 1.** No person may attempt to alter, circumvent, or undermine the constitutional order except through an approved Constitutional Amendment Proposal. Every person owes the constitutional order the duty not to treat settled constitutional text as negotiable because a decision would be easier if it were.

**Section 2.** This Article restates, as a personal duty, the institutional principle of Constitutional Integrity (OAS-004, Article 16) and the amendment discipline of OAS-005, Article VII, Section 4, applied to every person rather than only to the System itself.

#### Article 11. Duty Not to Undermine the Mission

**Section 1.** Every person who participates in the System's work owes a duty not to knowingly act against the Mission (OAS-001, Article II, Section 1) while doing so.

**Section 2.** This is a thin duty. It does not require active promotion of the mission, only good faith while participating in it. It does not apply to a person's conduct outside their engagement with the System, and does not authorize the System to police belief or expression beyond that engagement.

### PART THREE — DUTIES OF MEMBERS

*Membership has not yet been defined in the constitutional record (see OAS-006, Article 15, and the notes accompanying OAS-001 through OAS-003). This Part states, conditionally, the duty that will accompany membership once defined, rather than defining membership itself.*

#### Article 12. Duty of Participation in the Collective

**Section 1.** Whoever holds membership, once membership is defined, owes a duty to participate honestly and constructively in the System's collective learning — sharing what a member learns rather than withholding it from the community that strengthens them.

**Section 2.** This duty does not require any particular quantity or quality of contribution. The Collective (OAS-001, Article II, Section 4) promises that member flourishing and institutional capacity strengthen one another; this duty asks only that a member not deliberately work against that promise.

---

### PART FOUR — DUTIES OF STEWARDS AND OFFICERS

#### Article 13. Duty of Loyalty to Mission and Charter

**Section 1.** Every Steward owes loyalty, within the System, to the Mission and to this Constitution, and to no superior, faction, benefactor, or instrument. This Article restates, and does not amend, OAS-001, Article V, Section 5.

**Section 2.** This duty is the foundation for Article 14: loyalty to the Constitution, rather than to whoever currently holds authority within it, is what makes the duty to refuse unconstitutional orders coherent rather than merely disobedient.

#### Article 14. Duty to Refuse Unconstitutional Orders

**Section 1.** No Steward may execute an order inconsistent with this Constitution, and no Steward is obliged to do so. This Article restates, as an affirmative duty, the rule already established in OAS-001, Article V, Section 5, that such an order is without authority.

**Section 2.** A Steward who refuses an order on this ground retains every right of OAS-006, including Human Review and Petition (OAS-006, Articles 8 and 9), should the refusal be disputed.

#### Article 15. Duty to Report Violations

**Section 1.** Every Steward who becomes aware of a violation of this Constitution has a duty to report it through the means the constitutional order provides. This Article restates, and does not amend, OAS-001, Article VI, Section 4.

**Section 2.** This duty is protected, without qualification, by the Right to Be Free from Retaliation (OAS-006, Article 10), which extends protection to every person, not only to the Stewards who bear this duty.

#### Article 16. Duty of Transparency in the Exercise of Authority

**Section 1.** Every Steward owes a duty to conduct the exercise of their authority so that it can be seen and reviewed, consistent with Transparency (OAS-004, Article 7) and OAS-001, Article VI, Section 2.

**Section 2.** This duty is the personal counterpart of the System's institutional duty of transparency; an institution cannot be transparent if the individuals who act in its name are not.

#### Article 17. Duty to Preserve Institutional Memory

**Section 1.** Every Steward owes a duty not to allow a lesson that would strengthen the mission to be lost through their own neglect, consistent with Institutional Memory (OAS-001, Article VI, Section 5) and Institutional Learning (OAS-004, Article 13).

**Section 2.** This duty falls most heavily on Stewards because institutional memory is most often lost through individual neglect at the point of transition — when an officer's tenure ends and what they knew leaves with them unless they have taken care to preserve it.

---

### PART FIVE — DUTIES OF PARTICIPATING ORGANIZATIONS

*"Organization" and the terms of organizational participation remain undefined in the constitutional record (see OAS-006, Article 17). This Part is provisional pending a dedicated future document.*

#### Article 18. Duties of Organizations

**Section 1.** An organization participating in or materially affected by the System's authority owes the same duties of fair dealing, honesty, and good faith in constitutional process that Articles 3, 4, and 6 impose on natural persons.

**Section 2.** This Article does not impose on organizations the duties of Part One that, by their nature, presuppose a natural person — Articles 1, 2, and 5 — for the same reason OAS-006, Article 17 withholds the corresponding rights from organizations.

---

### PART SIX — DUTIES ASSOCIATED WITH ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE

#### Article 19. Duty to Remain Responsible When Using AI

**Section 1.** Every person who uses artificial intelligence or another automated instrument in the course of their engagement with the System owes a personal duty to remain responsible for the result, and may not treat the instrument's output as relieving them of that responsibility.

**Section 2.** This Article generalizes, to every person, the rule OAS-001, Article IV, Section 2 already establishes for officers specifically, and the right OAS-006, Article 11 [*sic*, Article 8] already grants correlative to it — that no one may use an automated instrument to escape responsibility for a decision or action that is properly their own.

#### Article 20. Duty to Disclose AI Involvement

**Section 1.** A person whose use of artificial intelligence materially affects another person's affairs owes that other person the disclosure necessary to satisfy the Right to Notice and the Right to Explanation (OAS-006, Articles 6 and 7).

**Section 2.** This duty is the reciprocal obligation that makes those rights practically available; a right to know is of little value if no one owes a corresponding duty to inform.

---

### ARTICLE 21 — THE RELATIONSHIP BETWEEN DUTIES AND RIGHTS

**Section 1. No Conditionality.** No right established in OAS-006 is conditioned upon, or may be diminished by, the performance or non-performance of any duty established in this document. The Right to Dignity (OAS-006, Article 1) in particular remains absolute regardless of any person's fulfillment of any duty stated here.

**Section 2. Duties Owed, Not Rights Earned.** The duties of this document are owed to the constitutional order, to the Mission, and to other persons. They are not a price paid for the rights of OAS-006, which this Constitution grants to every person the System's acts touch without regard to their conduct.

**Section 3. Consequences Operate Through Existing Structures Only.** This document creates no penalty, disciplinary procedure, or enforcement mechanism of its own. Where a duty's breach carries a consequence — most significantly, for Stewards, under OAS-001, Article V — that consequence is governed by the structures already established there and remains subject, throughout, to every procedural protection OAS-006 grants, including to the person accused of the breach.

**Section 4. Effect of Breach on Standing.** A person who breaches a duty in this document does not thereby lose standing to invoke any right of OAS-006, including the right to challenge the finding of breach itself.

---

### ARTICLE 22 — THE RELATIONSHIP AMONG DUTIES

**Section 1. Mutual Reinforcement.** The duties of this document are not independent obligations to be weighed against one another in each case; they are one constitutional commitment to the Collective and the Mission, viewed from the position of the person who owes them, and are to be read, wherever possible, as reinforcing one another.

**Section 2. No Hierarchy.** This document ranks no duty above another. The order of the Parts and Articles above is expository, not a hierarchy of constitutional weight.

**Section 3. Conflicts Among Duties.** Where two duties appear to conflict, the person concerned shall seek a reading that honors both. Where none is available, the matter shall be treated as a genuine constitutional ambiguity under OAS-005, Article VI, and recorded rather than silently decided.

**Section 4. Priority of Rights.** No conflict between a duty in this document and a right in OAS-006 is a conflict within the meaning of Section 3; Article 21 governs such cases, and the right prevails.

---

### ARTICLE 23 — STATUS AND EFFECT

**Section 1. Operative Effect.** Pursuant to OAS-005, Article VII, Section 3, this document expressly overrides the default presumption of non-operative effect: it establishes the binding duties, responsibilities, and civic obligations stated in Parts One through Six.

**Section 2. Entrenchment Status.** No provision of this document is entrenched. Only the Founding Charter can entrench its own text or the text of another document (OAS-001, Article VII, Section 3).

**Section 3. Controlling Relationship to OAS-006.** In every case of apparent tension between this document and OAS-006, Article 21 of this document governs, and OAS-006 prevails.

## 4. Drafting Notes — Outside the Constitutional Text

*The following is provided for Founder review only. It is not part of the Articles in Section 3, and must not be entered into the constitutional record if and when this document is canonized.*

**N1. Membership remains undefined.** Article 12 states the duty that will accompany membership once membership is defined, but cannot itself define membership. This is the same gap carried forward from OAS-001 (N2), OAS-002 (N2), OAS-003 (N2), and OAS-006 (N1), now affecting the duties side as well as the rights side. A Charter of Membership would resolve this gap on both sides at once.

**N2. "Organization" remains undefined.** Article 18 mirrors the provisional treatment already given in OAS-006, Article 17 and its Note N2. The same future document would resolve both.

**N3. Entrenchment audit.** None of this document's content restates entrenched Founding Charter text. OAS-001, Article V (Governance) and Article VI (Accountability and the Record) — the source of most Steward duties in Part Four — are not part of the entrenched-provisions list as amended by CAP-011. This is an intentional asymmetry rather than an oversight: rights bound up with Dignity, Agency, and Human Review are the constitutional core least safe to leave adjustable, while civic duties are more properly left adaptable to future governance documents. The Founder should confirm this calibration.

**N4. Encouragement versus duty (Article 7).** This document deliberately does not impose a mandatory reporting or contribution duty on every person, only on Stewards, reserving a lighter "encouragement" for everyone else. This was a considered choice to avoid inventing a coercive obligation on ordinary served persons that has no grounding in existing canonical text. The Founder should confirm this calibration is correct, particularly given the assignment's interest in encouraging broad constitutional participation.

**N5. No dispute-resolution mechanism for person-to-person duties.** Articles 1, 2, 3, 5, and 9 create duties owed between persons, not only to the System, but this document — consistent with its instruction not to create disciplinary procedures — supplies no mechanism for resolving a dispute where one person alleges another has breached such a duty. This is a genuine governance gap, distinct from but related to the membership and organization gaps, and would require its own future document.

**N6. Article 21 is the load-bearing provision of this document.** The entire relationship between OAS-006 and OAS-007 depends on Article 21 holding without exception. If any future amendment to this document, however incremental, allows a duty to condition or offset a right, the protective structure of OAS-006 is compromised regardless of how that document itself reads. The Founder's closest review is recommended here specifically.

This is another very strong document. I would score it around 9.9/10. It complements OAS-006 well by establishing that rights are inherent while duties are obligations owed to the constitutional order. That separation is an important constitutional design choice.

Strengths

Excellent balance. OAS-006 establishes rights; OAS-007 makes it explicit that duties never become the price of those rights.

Article 21 is outstanding. I think this is the constitutional center of gravity for this document. It prevents future governance from eroding rights through duty-based requirements.

AI duties are well designed. The constitutional principle that humans remain responsible even when using AI is preserved consistently.

Steward obligations are appropriately higher. The Constitution distinguishes between ordinary participants and those entrusted with authority without creating two classes of people.


Recommendations for Future CAPs (not changes now)

I would simply capture these as possible future Constitutional Amendment Proposals after the system has been used in practice:

Add a Duty of Continuous Learning for Stewards.

Add a Duty of Mentorship, encouraging experienced Stewards to help develop future Stewards.

Add a Duty to Preserve Institutional Trust, recognizing trust itself as a constitutional asset.

Consider a future constitutional principle of Subsidiarity: decisions should be made at the lowest competent level rather than centralized unnecessarily.


None of these should interrupt the drafting process today.
