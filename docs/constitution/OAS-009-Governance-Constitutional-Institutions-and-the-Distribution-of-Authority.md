# OAS-009 — Governance, Constitutional Institutions, and the Distribution of Authority

## 1. Document Title

**Governance, Constitutional Institutions, and the Distribution of Authority**
Canonical Designation: OAS-009
Status: Draft for Founder Review
Relationship to Canon: A constitutional document canonized under, subordinate to, and conformant with the Founding Charter (OAS-001), and consistent with OAS-002 through OAS-008. Governed by the default rules of OAS-005, Article VII, except where this document expressly provides otherwise.

## 2. Purpose

This document establishes the governance architecture the Founding Charter requires but does not itself supply: the constitutional institutions that hold, separate, and check the System's authority, as OAS-001, Article V anticipates, and the offices to which OAS-005, Article I, Section 22 already deferred. It establishes three coequal constitutional bodies implementing the separation of powers already required by OAS-001, Article V, Section 2; a custodial office protecting the constitutional record; and the mechanism — though not the act — by which the Founder's founding-period authority may eventually transfer to durable institutional bodies, as OAS-001, Article V, Section 4 already promises. It establishes architecture only, not elections, staffing, or administration, which remain reserved to subordinate documents.

## 3. Draft Constitutional Text

---

### PART ONE — PRINCIPLES OF CONSTITUTIONAL GOVERNANCE

#### Article 1. Government Under the Charter

**Section 1.** All governance of the System is exercised under this Constitution and is limited by it. This Article restates, and does not amend, OAS-001, Article V, Section 1.

**Section 2.** This document supplies the composition, distribution, and structure of authority that OAS-001, Article V, Section 1 reserves to subordinate constitutional documents. It does not supply the further operational detail — selection procedures, staffing, and administration — which remains reserved to non-constitutional governance instruments.

#### Article 2. Separation of Powers Implemented

**Section 1.** OAS-001, Article V, Section 2 requires the System to separate, at minimum, the authority to set direction and canon, the authority to execute and administer, and the authority to review conduct against this Constitution, and forbids any single person from permanently holding all three. This document implements that requirement through the three constitutional bodies established in Part Two.

**Section 2.** This Article does not amend OAS-001, Article V, Section 2. It supplies the institutional form that Section already required to exist.

#### Article 3. Written Authority Only

**Section 1.** No constitutional body or office established by this document holds any authority beyond what this document, or a document lawfully amending it, grants in writing. This Article restates, and does not amend, OAS-001, Article V, Section 3.

**Section 2.** Every office within the bodies of Part Two shall be created, and its authority stated, in the manner Section 1 requires, whether by this document directly or by a subordinate document canonized under it.

---

### PART TWO — THE THREE CONSTITUTIONAL BODIES

#### Article 4. The Council

**Section 1.** The Council holds the authority to set direction and canon for the System: to approve Constitutional Amendment Proposals, canonize constitutional documents, and resolve constitutional questions this Constitution reserves to that authority. The Council is the durable institutional body to which the Founder's canonization authority (OAS-001, Article V, Section 4) is designed to transfer, as Article 16 of this document provides.

**Section 2.** The Council may not execute or administer the System's operations, and may not serve as the final reviewer of its own conduct against this Constitution; those authorities belong to the Administration and the Tribunal, respectively (Articles 5 and 6).

#### Article 5. The Administration

**Section 1.** The Administration holds the authority to execute and administer the System's operations in service of the Mission, within the bounds the Council's canon and the Tribunal's review establish.

**Section 2.** The Administration may not set canon, approve Constitutional Amendment Proposals, or serve as the final reviewer of its own conduct against this Constitution; those authorities belong to the Council and the Tribunal, respectively.

#### Article 6. The Tribunal

**Section 1.** The Tribunal holds the authority to review the conduct of every officer, body, and instrument of the System against this Constitution, including the Council and the Administration. It is the body through which the Right of Petition and Redress (OAS-006, Article 9), the Right to Human Review (OAS-006, Article 8), and the ambiguity-recording duties of OAS-005, Article VI are principally exercised.

**Section 2.** The Tribunal may not set canon or execute the System's operations. A Tribunal finding that a provision requires amendment is a recommendation to the Council through the Constitutional Amendment Proposal process, and is not itself an amendment.

#### Article 7. Composition and Selection

**Section 1.** Each of the Council, the Administration, and the Tribunal shall be composed of more than one Steward, so that no single person permanently holds the authority of any one body, consistent with OAS-001, Article V, Section 2.

**Section 2.** The specific size, selection process, and term of service for each body's Stewards shall be established by subordinate governance documents, and shall at minimum provide for regular renewal of membership over time and a defined maximum tenure, so that no Steward holds a body's authority indefinitely.

#### Article 8. Removal and Accountability of Institutional Officers

**Section 1.** A Steward serving on the Council, the Administration, or the Tribunal may be removed from that office only for cause, only through a process established by subordinate governance documents, and only consistent with the procedural protections OAS-006 and OAS-007 already guarantee to every Steward (OAS-007, Article 16, Section 2).

**Section 2.** No body may serve as the sole and final authority over the removal of its own members. A Steward facing removal from the Council or the Administration holds the right to review by the Tribunal. A Steward facing removal from the Tribunal holds the right to review by the Council, exercised only for this narrow purpose and not as a general grant of conduct-review authority over the Tribunal's ordinary decisions.

---

### PART THREE — THE CONSTITUTIONAL RECORD

#### Article 9. The Office of the Constitutional Record

**Section 1.** The System shall maintain a standing office responsible for the permanent constitutional record required by OAS-001, Article VI, Section 1, and for verifying that no canonical document is entered, amended, or altered except through an approved Constitutional Amendment Proposal.

**Section 2.** This office holds no authority to set canon, execute operations, or review conduct on the merits; its function is custodial. It is accountable to the Tribunal for the integrity of the record it keeps, and its findings that a purported change lacks an approved Constitutional Amendment Proposal are conclusive unless overturned by the Tribunal.

---

### PART FOUR — COOPERATION AND INDEPENDENCE AMONG INSTITUTIONS

#### Article 10. Comity Among Constitutional Bodies

**Section 1.** The Council, the Administration, and the Tribunal shall each respect the constitutional domain of the others and shall cooperate in good faith toward the Mission, consistent with the Duty of Good Faith (OAS-007, Article 6) applied among institutions rather than only among persons.

**Section 2.** Cooperation under this Article does not permit one body to exercise another's authority, even temporarily or by mutual agreement. The separation established by OAS-001, Article V, Section 2 may be adjusted only by amendment to the Founding Charter itself, never by inter-body agreement.

#### Article 11. Jurisdictional Disputes

**Section 1.** A dispute between constitutional bodies about which holds authority over a given matter shall be resolved under OAS-005, Article III, in the same manner as a conflict between constitutional documents, since each body's authority derives from constitutional text.

**Section 2.** Pending resolution of such a dispute, the body that has most recently exercised authority over the matter shall continue to do so, to avoid a governance vacuum while the dispute is resolved.

---

### PART FIVE — DELEGATION OF AUTHORITY

#### Article 12. Principles of Delegation

**Section 1.** Any Steward holding authority under this document may delegate a specific, bounded portion of that authority to another person, provided the delegation is in writing, states its scope and duration, and remains revocable by the delegating Steward.

**Section 2.** Delegation does not transfer accountability. The delegating Steward remains accountable for the delegated authority's exercise, consistent with Stewardship (OAS-004, Article 8) and Responsibility (OAS-004, Article 11); a Steward may not delegate away the duty to answer for how authority entrusted to them is used.

#### Article 13. Non-Delegable Authorities

**Section 1.** The Council's authority to approve Constitutional Amendment Proposals, and the Tribunal's authority to render final constitutional review, may never be delegated to any person or instrument outside those bodies, including to artificial intelligence, consistent with Reserved Human Judgment (OAS-001, Article IV, Section 3) and Human–AI Stewardship (OAS-004, Article 12).

**Section 2.** This Article exists because the two authorities named in Section 1 are the ones most capable of altering the constitutional order itself. Delegating them, even temporarily, would create exactly the concentration of power OAS-001, Article V, Section 2 was written to prevent.

---

### PART SIX — SUCCESSION, CONTINUITY, AND TRANSITION FROM THE FOUNDING PERIOD

#### Article 14. Continuity of Institutions

**Section 1.** No constitutional body may be reduced to a state in which none of its Stewards can act. Subordinate governance documents shall establish a minimum quorum and a line of succession sufficient to prevent any body from becoming entirely vacant.

**Section 2.** This Article implements OAS-001, Article VIII, Section 1 for the specific institutions this document establishes.

#### Article 15. Emergency Continuity

**Section 1.** Where a crisis prevents the Council, the Administration, or the Tribunal from functioning through its ordinary composition, any emergency authority exercised in its place must satisfy OAS-001, Article VIII, Section 2 in full: limited in scope, limited in duration, subject to review, and incapable of amending the constitutional order.

**Section 2.** This Article creates no new emergency authority. It confirms that the limits already established in the Founding Charter apply without exception to the institutions this document establishes.

#### Article 16. Transition from the Founding Period

**Section 1.** The founding period described in OAS-001, Article V, Section 4 ends when the Founder approves a Constitutional Amendment Proposal expressly declaring it ended. This is the sole mechanism by which it may end; no other act, passage of time, or inference may end it.

**Section 2.** Upon approval of such a Constitutional Amendment Proposal, the Founder's authority to approve and canonize constitutional documents, and to resolve constitutional questions the Founding Charter reserves to Founder approval, passes to the Council, to be exercised under whatever decision procedure a subordinate governance document establishes, consistent with this document's requirement that no single person hold that authority alone thereafter.

**Section 3.** This Article does not itself end the founding period and does not alter the Founder's present authority under OAS-001, Article V, Section 4. It establishes, for the first time, the specific mechanism and destination the Founding Charter already promised — "the orderly transfer of the Founder's constitutional authorities to durable institutional bodies" — without exercising that transfer itself.

**Section 4.** A Constitutional Amendment Proposal under Section 1 may, as part of the same proposal, establish transitional arrangements, including a period during which the Founder retains a defined and limited role, or a phased transfer of specific authorities before others, consistent with the requirement that the transfer be orderly rather than abrupt.

---

### ARTICLE 17 — RELATIONSHIP AMONG GOVERNANCE PROVISIONS

**Section 1. Mutual Reinforcement.** The institutions and rules of this document are not independent grants of power to be read in isolation; they are one structure implementing the separation and accountability OAS-001, Article V already requires, and are to be read together.

**Section 2. No Hierarchy Among the Three Bodies.** The Council, the Administration, and the Tribunal are coequal in constitutional standing. None is superior to another in general; each is supreme only within the domain OAS-001, Article V, Section 2 assigns it.

**Section 3. Priority of Rights and Duties.** Nothing in this document diminishes any right established in OAS-006 or alters any duty established in OAS-007. Every institution and officer this document establishes is bound by both in full.

**Section 4. Conflicts.** Conflicts not otherwise resolved by this Article are resolved under OAS-005, Articles III and VI.

---

### ARTICLE 18 — STATUS AND EFFECT

**Section 1. Operative Effect.** Pursuant to OAS-005, Article VII, Section 3, this document expressly overrides the default presumption of non-operative effect: it establishes the constitutional bodies, offices, and authorities described in Parts Two through Six.

**Section 2. Entrenchment Status.** No provision of this document is entrenched. Only the Founding Charter can entrench its own text (OAS-001, Article VII, Section 3); see Drafting Notes, N1, regarding the entrenchment status of the separation-of-powers requirement this document implements.

**Section 3. Fulfillment of Prior Forward References.** This document is the "subordinate governance document under OAS-001, Article V" that OAS-005, Article I, Section 22 anticipated when defining "Steward," and supplies, for the Council specifically, the mechanism OAS-005, Article I, Section 10 anticipated for resolving how the Founder's authority may be transferred.

## 4. Drafting Notes — Outside the Constitutional Text

*The following is provided for Founder review only. It is not part of the Articles in Section 3, and must not be entered into the constitutional record if and when this document is canonized.*

**N1. Recommended CAP: entrench the separation of powers itself.** OAS-001, Article V — including Section 2's separation-of-powers requirement, which this entire document implements — is not on the entrenched-provisions list as amended by CAP-011. This means the abstract requirement this document's whole architecture rests on could, in principle, be weakened by a future amendment to OAS-001, Article V without touching any entrenched text. This is the most significant structural vulnerability this document surfaces. A future Constitutional Amendment Proposal extending entrenchment to OAS-001, Article V, Section 2 is strongly recommended; this document does not take that action, since only the Founding Charter can entrench its own text.

**N2. This document closes a forward reference, not a new gap.** OAS-005, Article I, Section 22 stated that formal offices are "reserved to subordinate governance documents under OAS-001, Article V." This document is that reservation fulfilled. Article 18, Section 3 records this explicitly.

**N3. Founder transition is deliberately half-resolved.** Article 16 establishes the mechanism and destination for the transfer of Founder authority, answering the question OAS-005, Article I, Section 10 left open and closing the item flagged as most urgent since OAS-001's own Notes (N4). It deliberately does not trigger that transfer, since only the Founder holds the authority to end the founding period, consistent with OAS-001, Article V, Section 4. The Founder should treat Article 16 as the mechanism now available, to be invoked at a time of the Founder's own choosing.

**N4. The Tribunal-removal design is a judgment call.** Article 8, Section 2 gives the Council a narrow role in reviewing the removal of Tribunal members, to avoid the Tribunal being the sole judge of its own membership. Alternative designs were possible — an external body, or a supermajority requirement across both other bodies — and this document chose the simplest workable option. The Founder should confirm this design or direct an alternative before this Article is relied upon.

**N5. The Office of the Constitutional Record holds genuinely new authority.** Article 9, Section 2's rule that the Office's procedural findings are "conclusive unless overturned by the Tribunal" is the first grant, anywhere in canon, of a presumptively binding institutional finding. It is narrowly scoped to procedural questions (whether an approved CAP exists for a given change) rather than substantive canon, but it is new law, not restatement, and the Founder's specific attention is recommended here.

**N6. Non-delegability to AI (Article 13) is an extension, not a restatement.** The underlying principle — that final constitutional authority cannot be delegated to an automated instrument — rests on entrenched text (OAS-001, Article IV, Section 3), but its specific application to Council and Tribunal authority is stated here for the first time and is not itself entrenched.

**N7. Recommended CAP: connect Membership to institutional selection.** This document deliberately left the selection procedures for Council, Administration, and Tribunal Stewards to future subordinate documents, consistent with the instruction to establish architecture rather than election rules. OAS-008, Article 15 already promises Members "a meaningful opportunity to participate in the System's Institutional Learning"; a future document connecting that promise to a formal role in selecting institutional Stewards is recommended as a priority, though not undertaken here.
