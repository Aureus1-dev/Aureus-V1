# OAS-006 — Rights, Human Dignity, Agency, and Constitutional Protections

## 1. Document Title

**Rights, Human Dignity, Agency, and Constitutional Protections**
Canonical Designation: OAS-006
Status: Draft for Founder Review
Relationship to Canon: A constitutional document canonized under, subordinate to, and conformant with the Founding Charter (OAS-001), and consistent with OAS-002 through OAS-005. Governed by the default rules of OAS-005, Article VII, except where this document expressly provides otherwise.

## 2. Purpose

This document establishes the enduring rights, protections, and guarantees owed to every person by the Open Advancement System. Where a right restates a principle already established in OAS-001 or OAS-004, this document translates that principle from an institutional duty into a person-held claim, without amending its source. Where a right is established here for the first time, it is identified as such. This document is expressly operative: pursuant to OAS-005, Article VII, Section 3, it grants enforceable rights rather than merely declaring conviction, and that default is overridden accordingly.

## 3. Draft Constitutional Text

---

### PART ONE — UNIVERSAL RIGHTS OF THE PERSON

*The rights of this Part are held by every person the System serves, employs, governs, or otherwise affects, without regard to membership, office, or any other relationship to the System.*

#### Article 1. The Right to Dignity

**Section 1.** Every person holds the right to be treated by the System as an end in themselves, and never merely as a means to the System's purposes. This right restates, and does not amend, the entrenched principle of Human Dignity (OAS-001, Article III, Section 1; OAS-004, Article 1).

**Section 2.** This right is absolute. It admits no balancing against efficiency, mission progress, the flourishing of others, or any other right in this document. Where doubt exists whether an act of the System treats a person merely as a means, that doubt is resolved against the System (see Article 13).

#### Article 2. The Right to Agency

**Section 1.** Every person holds the right to understand, decide, and act for themselves in matters materially affecting them, and the right not to have that capacity displaced by the System's assistance. This right restates, and does not amend, the entrenched principle of Human Agency (OAS-001, Article III, Section 2; OAS-004, Article 2).

**Section 2.** This right does not entitle a person to demand the System withhold help; it entitles them to help that strengthens their own judgment rather than substitutes for it. It is closely linked to the rights of Part Two, which govern how the System must act when it exercises authority over a person's affairs.

#### Article 3. The Right to Fair Treatment

**Section 1.** Every person holds the right to fair and equal regard by the System's authority, whatever their standing, station, or relationship to the institution. This right translates the principle of Justice (OAS-004, Article 3) into a person-held claim. Justice is operative but, unlike Articles 1 and 2, is not entrenched text of the Founding Charter.

**Section 2.** This right requires that the System's rules be applied as written and that like cases be treated alike. It is closely bound to Accountability (Article 10) and to the procedural protections of Part Two, without which a claim of unfair treatment could not be examined.

#### Article 4. The Right to Truthful Treatment

**Section 1.** Every person holds the right not to be deceived by the System, and the right to a candid account of the System's own errors where those errors affect them. This right translates Truth (OAS-004, Article 5) and Integrity (OAS-001, Article III, Section 4) into a person-held claim.

**Section 2.** This right is distinct from the Right to Explanation (Article 7): truthful treatment concerns whether what the System says is honest; explanation concerns whether what the System says is intelligible. A truthful account that is incomprehensible does not satisfy Article 7, and an intelligible account that is false does not satisfy this Article.

#### Article 5. The Right to Privacy

**Section 1.** Every person holds the right to control the disclosure of their own personal information, and the right to know what the System holds concerning them and to seek its correction. This right is established here for the first time; it has no prior statement elsewhere in the constitutional record, though it is grounded in Human Dignity's prohibition on instrumentalizing a person (Article 1) and in Human Agency's protection of self-determination (Article 2).

**Section 2.** This right does not forbid the System from holding or using personal information necessary to its mission; it forbids collection, use, or disclosure beyond what the mission requires, and reserves to the person the right to know and to correct what is held. This right is not entrenched, and the Founder should consider whether it requires fuller, dedicated treatment beyond what this Article provides (see Drafting Notes, N5).

---

### PART TWO — PROCEDURAL PROTECTIONS

*The rights of this Part govern how the System must act whenever it exercises authority over a person's affairs, whether or not artificial intelligence is involved in the decision.*

#### Article 6. The Right to Notice

**Section 1.** A person materially affected by an act of the System's authority holds the right to be informed that the act has occurred, in terms sufficient to allow them to exercise the rights of this Part. This right is established here for the first time; it is a necessary precondition for the Right of Petition and Redress already established in OAS-001, Article VI, Section 3, which cannot meaningfully be exercised by a person unaware that a decision affecting them was made.

**Section 2.** Notice may be delayed or limited only where the System's own safety principles require an exception, consistent with the limits already placed on Openness (OAS-001, Article III, Section 3) and Transparency (OAS-004, Article 7); any such exception must be justified and recorded rather than assumed.

#### Article 7. The Right to Explanation

**Section 1.** A person materially affected by an act of the System's authority holds the right to an intelligible account of the basis of that act. Where the act was informed by artificial intelligence or another automated instrument, this right restates the entitlement already established in OAS-001, Article IV, Section 4, which is operative but not entrenched.

**Section 2.** This right requires an account a reasonable person can understand, not a technical record only a specialist could interpret. It does not require the System to disclose information that would itself violate another person's rights under this document or compromise the System's own safety principles.

#### Article 8. The Right to Human Review

**Section 1.** A person materially affected by a decision of the System holds the right to have that decision reviewed by an accountable human being, and the right to have no such decision stand as final on the authority of an automated instrument alone. This right restates the entrenched principle of Reserved Human Judgment (OAS-001, Article IV, Section 3) and is, to that extent, itself protected by entrenchment.

**Section 2.** This right is the person-held counterpart of Human–AI Stewardship (OAS-004, Article 12): where that principle binds the System not to let an instrument carry constitutional responsibility, this Article gives the affected person a standing claim to invoke that binding.

#### Article 9. The Right of Petition and Redress

**Section 1.** Every person materially affected by an act of the System holds the right to petition for its review and to seek a genuine remedy, administered by accountable human beings. This right restates, and does not amend, OAS-001, Article VI, Section 3, which remains operative but not entrenched.

**Section 2.** This right is the practical mechanism through which every other right in this Part becomes more than aspiration. It depends on the Right to Notice (Article 6) to be invoked knowingly, and on Human Review (Article 8) to be resolved by an accountable person rather than by the same authority whose act is being challenged.

#### Article 10. The Right to Be Free from Retaliation

**Section 1.** No person may be penalized, disadvantaged, or retaliated against by the System for exercising any right established in this document, in good faith. This right generalizes the narrower protection already established in OAS-001, Article VI, Section 4, which protects only officers reporting a violation of the Charter; this Article extends the same protection to any person exercising any right under this document, and is established as new operative text to that extent.

**Section 2.** A claim under this Article is itself subject to the Right of Petition and Redress (Article 9) and may not be dismissed by the same authority accused of the retaliation.

---

### PART THREE — LIMITS ON INSTITUTIONAL POWER

#### Article 11. No Authority Without Grant

**Section 1.** The System may exercise only the authority granted to it in writing by this Constitution or a document lawfully canonized under it. This Article restates, and does not amend, OAS-001, Article V, Section 3.

**Section 2.** This limit is the structural precondition for every right in this document: a right against an unlimited power is no protection at all. It is reinforced by Constitutional Integrity (Article 12) and by the presumption of Article 13.

#### Article 12. No Silent Constitutional Change

**Section 1.** No right established in this document may be narrowed, removed, or reinterpreted except through an approved Constitutional Amendment Proposal. This Article restates, and does not amend, Constitutional Integrity (OAS-004, Article 16) and the amendment rules of OAS-005, Article VII, Section 4.

**Section 2.** This Article exists because rights that can be quietly reinterpreted away in practice are not rights but expectations, and this Constitution has already rejected that possibility for every canonical document, not only for this one.

#### Article 13. The Presumption Against Instrumentalization

**Section 1.** Where it is genuinely unclear whether an act of the System treats a person as a means rather than as an end, that doubt shall be resolved against the System. This Article is established here for the first time, as an operative extension of Human Dignity's absolute status (Article 1) and the general ambiguity rule of OAS-005, Article VI, Section 1.

**Section 2.** This presumption does not apply to genuine, good-faith disagreements about policy or method; it applies only where the question is whether a person's dignity has been put at risk, and exists to ensure that such questions are never resolved by default in the System's own favor.

---

### PART FOUR — RIGHTS BY RELATIONSHIP TO THE SYSTEM

#### Article 14. Scope of Application

**Section 1.** The rights of Parts One, Two, and Three apply to every person the System's acts touch, regardless of whether that person also falls within Article 15, 16, or 17. The categories that follow add to those universal rights; they never subtract from them, and no person may be denied a universal right on the ground that they do not fall within any of those categories.

**Section 2.** Where this Article and a more specific provision of this Part appear to conflict, Article 19 governs.

#### Article 15. Rights of Members

**Section 1.** This Constitution has not yet defined who holds membership in the System; that definition is reserved to a future constitutional document, as OAS-001's Notes (N2), OAS-002's Notes (N2), and OAS-003's Notes (N2) have each already flagged. This Article therefore states, conditionally, the rights that attach to membership once defined, rather than defining membership itself.

**Section 2.** Whoever holds membership, once membership is defined, holds — in addition to the universal rights of Parts One through Three — the benefit of the Collective principle (OAS-001, Article II, Section 4): that their flourishing and the System's capacity to serve others are never treated as competing, and that they share, as both contributor and beneficiary, in the System's Institutional Learning (OAS-001, Article III, Section 8).

#### Article 16. Rights of Stewards and Officers

**Section 1.** Every Steward, as defined in OAS-005, Article I, Section 22, holds — in addition to the universal rights of Parts One through Three — the right to know, in writing, the authority they are granted and the standard by which their exercise of it may be reviewed, consistent with OAS-001, Article V, Section 3.

**Section 2.** A Steward facing removal or discipline for the exercise of their office holds the same procedural protections this document guarantees to any person materially affected by the System's authority — Notice, Explanation, Human Review, and Petition (Articles 6 through 9) — applied to the Steward's own standing. This does not diminish the Accountability the Steward owes as a holder of authority (OAS-001, Article III, Section 5); it ensures that Accountability is exercised through fair process rather than at will.

#### Article 17. Rights of Participating Organizations

**Section 1.** Where an organization, rather than a natural person, participates in or is materially affected by the System's authority, it holds the procedural protections of Part Two (Articles 6 through 9) to the same extent a natural person would.

**Section 2.** An organization does not hold the rights of Part One that this Constitution reserves, by their nature, to natural persons — most fundamentally, Human Dignity and Human Agency (OAS-001, Article III, Sections 1 and 2), which speak throughout the constitutional record of "every person" in a sense inherently limited to human beings. This Constitution has not yet defined "organization" or the terms of organizational participation in the System; that definition, like membership (Article 15), is reserved to a future constitutional document, and this Article should be read as provisional pending it.

#### Article 18. Entrenchment Status of Rights in AI-Assisted Decisions

**Section 1.** The Right to Human Review (Article 8), insofar as it rests on OAS-001, Article IV, Section 3, restates entrenched constitutional text and may not be weakened by any future amendment to this document alone; weakening it would require amending the Founding Charter itself.

**Section 2.** The Right to Explanation (Article 7), insofar as it rests on OAS-001, Article IV, Section 4, restates operative but non-entrenched text, and could in principle be narrowed by a future amendment to this document, subject always to the Founding Charter's supremacy and to Article 12 of this document.

---

### ARTICLE 19 — THE RELATIONSHIP AMONG RIGHTS AND LIMITS

**Section 1. Mutual Reinforcement.** The rights and limits of this document are not independent claims to be weighed against one another in each case; they are one constitutional commitment to human dignity and agency, viewed from the position of the person who holds them, and are to be read, wherever possible, as reinforcing one another.

**Section 2. Dignity as Floor.** The Right to Dignity (Article 1) is never subject to balancing against any other right in this document, against the Mission, or against any institutional interest. Every other right in this document may, in a genuine conflict, be weighed against the Mission or against another person's rights; the Right to Dignity may not.

**Section 3. Conflicts Among Persons' Rights.** Where the rights of two persons conflict, the officer or body responsible shall first seek a reading that honors both. Where none is available, the matter shall be treated as a genuine constitutional ambiguity under OAS-005, Article VI, and recorded rather than silently decided.

**Section 4. No Hierarchy Beyond Dignity.** Except as Section 2 provides for the Right to Dignity, this document ranks no right above another. The order of the Parts and Articles above is expository, not a hierarchy of constitutional weight.

---

### ARTICLE 20 — STATUS AND EFFECT

**Section 1. Operative Effect.** Pursuant to OAS-005, Article VII, Section 3, this document expressly overrides the default presumption of non-operative effect: it grants the enforceable rights, protections, and guarantees stated in Parts One through Four to the persons and entities each Part and Article describes.

**Section 2. Entrenchment Status.** Except where an Article of this document expressly identifies itself as restating entrenched text of the Founding Charter (Articles 1, 2, and 8, and Article 18, Section 1), no right established for the first time in this document is itself entrenched. Only the Founding Charter can entrench its own text or the text of another document (OAS-001, Article VII, Section 3).

## 4. Drafting Notes — Outside the Constitutional Text

*The following is provided for Founder review only. It is not part of the Articles in Section 3, and must not be entered into the constitutional record if and when this document is canonized.*

**N1. Membership remains undefined.** Article 15 states the rights that will attach to membership once membership is defined, but does not and cannot define membership itself. This is the same gap flagged in OAS-001 (N2), OAS-002 (N2), and OAS-003 (N2), now made more consequential: a person cannot know today whether Article 15 applies to them. A Charter of Membership is the most urgent outstanding drafting need this document surfaces.

**N2. "Organization" is a wholly new, undefined category.** Article 17 was drafted narrowly and conditionally because no prior canonical document defines what an "organization participating in OAS" is, how such participation is established, or what obligations accompany it. The Article extends only the procedural protections that follow logically from existing principles (fairness, accountability) and expressly withholds Part One's person-specific rights. The Founder should treat Article 17 as provisional pending a dedicated foundational document, in the same manner as Article 15.

**N3. New rights created by this subordinate document are not entrenched.** Article 20, Section 2 makes this explicit, but it deserves emphasis: the Right to Privacy (Article 5), the Right to Notice (Article 6), the broadened Right to Be Free from Retaliation (Article 10), and the Presumption Against Instrumentalization (Article 13) are all established here for the first time and are, as a structural matter, less protected than the rights that restate entrenched Founding Charter text. A future amendment to this document — properly approved through the CAP process — could in principle narrow them without touching the Founding Charter at all. If the Founder regards any of these as foundational enough to warrant entrenchment, that requires a separate amendment to OAS-001, Article VII, Section 3, which only the Founding Charter can accomplish.

**N4. The broadened anti-retaliation right (Article 10) is a genuine expansion, not a restatement.** OAS-001, Article VI, Section 4 protects only officers who report a Charter violation. Article 10 of this document extends protection to any person exercising any right in this entire document. This was a drafting judgment made to close an obvious gap — a right to petition is of little value if petitioning can be punished — but it is new law, not reconciliation, and the Founder should confirm it was intended at this breadth.

**N5. The Right to Privacy is thin and may need dedicated treatment.** Article 5 establishes privacy at the level of constitutional principle only, deliberately avoiding operational detail (retention periods, consent mechanics, data categories) that would belong in policy rather than constitutional text. Given how central data handling is likely to be to the System's actual operations, the Founder should consider whether privacy warrants its own foundational document rather than a single Article here.

**N6. Entrenchment precision.** Articles 1, 2, and 8 (and Article 18, Section 1) were verified against the current entrenched-provisions list as amended by CAP-011: OAS-001, Article III, Sections 1, 2, and 5 (Dignity, Agency, Accountability), Article III, Section 6 (Stewardship), and Article IV, Sections 2 and 3 (Human Accountability, Reserved Human Judgment) are entrenched; Article IV, Sections 4 and 5, Article III, Sections 3, 4, and 7, and all of OAS-004's newly established principles (including Justice) are not. This document's entrenchment claims track that list precisely; if CAP-011 is not approved as drafted, every entrenchment claim in this document should be re-checked against whatever list is actually approved.
