# OAS-010 — Constitutional Amendment Process, Canonization, and Stewardship of the Constitutional Record

## 1. Document Title

**Constitutional Amendment Process, Canonization, and Stewardship of the Constitutional Record**
Canonical Designation: OAS-010
Status: Draft for Founder Review
Relationship to Canon: A constitutional document canonized under, subordinate to, and conformant with the Founding Charter (OAS-001), and consistent with OAS-002 through OAS-009. Governed by the default rules of OAS-005, Article VII, except where this document expressly provides otherwise.

## 2. Purpose

This document establishes the complete process by which constitutional change occurs: how a Constitutional Amendment Proposal is formed, submitted, reviewed, approved, canonized, and preserved, and how repeal, supersession, and correction are distinguished and handled. It completes the definition OAS-005, Article I, Section 7 deferred, and gives operative procedure to Constitutional Integrity (OAS-004, Article 16), which this entire constitutional record has followed as informal practice since its first document. It formalizes that practice; it does not change it.

## 3. Draft Constitutional Text

---

### PART ONE — THE CONSTITUTIONAL AMENDMENT PROPOSAL

#### Article 1. What a CAP Is

**Section 1.** A Constitutional Amendment Proposal, or CAP, is the sole instrument by which any change to Canon — new constitutional text, an amendment to existing text, a correction, a repeal, or a supersession — may be proposed. This Article completes the definition OAS-005, Article I, Section 7 established and expressly deferred to this document.

**Section 2.** The Canonizing Authority is whoever currently holds the authority to approve Constitutional Amendment Proposals under OAS-001, Article V, Section 4: the Founder, during the founding period, or the Council, from the transition described in OAS-009, Article 16 onward. This document uses "Canonizing Authority" throughout to mean whichever of these currently holds that authority, so that this document does not require amendment upon transition.

**Section 3.** A CAP has no canonical effect until approved by the Canonizing Authority and entered into the constitutional record by the Office of the Constitutional Record (OAS-009, Article 9). Before that point, a CAP is a proposal only, whatever its content or quality.

#### Article 2. Required Contents of a CAP

**Section 1.** Every CAP shall state: a unique designation number; a title; the date of submission; the originating session or context in which the proposal arose; the constitutional documents it relates to; the current constitutional text it would affect, if any; the proposed constitutional text; the reason for the proposed change; its expected constitutional impact; any potential risks or tradeoffs; its dependencies on other CAPs; a recommendation; and its current status under Article 4.

**Section 2.** A CAP lacking any element of Section 1 is incomplete and may not be canonized, though it may proceed to review under Article 5 for the purpose of being completed. This Article formalizes, as constitutional requirement, the template already in use in this constitutional record's own practice.

#### Article 3. Who May Submit a CAP

**Section 1.** Any person may submit a CAP proposing a constitutional change, consistent with the civic participation this Constitution encourages (OAS-007, Article 7) and the Duty to Preserve Space for Disagreement (OAS-007, Article 8).

**Section 2.** The right to submit a CAP is not a right to its approval. Submission places a proposal into the review process of Article 5; only the Canonizing Authority may approve it.

---

### PART TWO — THE CAP LIFECYCLE

#### Article 4. CAP Status Categories

**Section 1.** Every CAP holds, at any time, one of the following statuses: **Draft**, meaning the proposal is being prepared and has not been submitted for review; **Under Review**, meaning the proposal has been submitted and is undergoing the review of Article 5; **Approved**, meaning the Canonizing Authority has approved the proposal but it has not yet been entered into the constitutional record; **Rejected**, meaning the Canonizing Authority has declined to approve it; **Withdrawn**, meaning the proposal's submitter has withdrawn it before a decision; and **Canonized**, meaning the proposal has been approved and entered into the constitutional record under Article 7.

**Section 2.** A CAP's status shall be recorded and preserved permanently regardless of its outcome, consistent with Article 15. A Rejected or Withdrawn CAP remains part of the historical record and may be resubmitted, in the same or amended form, at any time.

#### Article 5. Stages of Review

**Section 1.** A submitted CAP shall pass through: constitutional conflict review under Article 6; a period of deliberation proportionate to the significance of the proposed change, during which the proposal is available for examination by any person; and a decision by the Canonizing Authority to approve, reject, or return the proposal for revision.

**Section 2.** This Article establishes the stages every CAP must pass through. It does not establish their exact duration or administration, which remain reserved to subordinate governance documents as operational matters.

#### Article 6. Constitutional Conflict Review

**Section 1.** Before a CAP reaches the Canonizing Authority for decision, the Tribunal shall review it for conflict with entrenched provisions of the Founding Charter and for consistency with the rest of Canon, and shall report any conflict it finds.

**Section 2.** A Tribunal finding of conflict with a non-entrenched provision is advisory to the Canonizing Authority, which retains the decision whether to approve, revise, or reject the CAP. A Tribunal finding that a CAP would directly contradict an entrenched provision identifies that the CAP, if approved in that form, would be void under OAS-001, Article VII, Section 3. This Article does not grant the Tribunal power to reject a CAP the Canonizing Authority still wishes to approve, but the Office of the Constitutional Record may not enter such a CAP into the record as valid canon (Article 7, Section 2).

---

### PART THREE — CANONIZATION

#### Article 7. The Act of Canonization

**Section 1.** Canonization occurs when the Canonizing Authority approves a CAP and the Office of the Constitutional Record enters its proposed text into the permanent constitutional record. Both steps are required; approval alone does not canonize a change.

**Section 2.** The Office of the Constitutional Record shall refuse to enter a CAP that would, on its face, directly contradict an entrenched provision of the Founding Charter, consistent with Article 6, Section 2, and shall record its refusal and the reasons for it as part of the permanent record.

#### Article 8. Effective Date

**Section 1.** A canonized change takes effect immediately upon entry into the constitutional record, unless the CAP itself states a different effective date.

**Section 2.** No canonized change has retroactive effect on a decision or act properly taken under the constitutional text in force at the time of that decision or act, unless the CAP expressly and narrowly provides otherwise.

#### Article 9. Document Status After Canonization

**Section 1.** A constitutional document's status progresses from Draft, before any CAP proposing its canonization is approved, to Canonical, once such a CAP is approved and entered under Article 7.

**Section 2.** A Canonical document remains Canonical through every subsequent amendment to it; amendment changes a document's text, not its canonical status. A document ceases to be Canonical only by becoming Repealed, Superseded, or Archived, as Part Four provides.

---

### PART FOUR — REPEAL, SUPERSESSION, CORRECTION, AND ARCHIVAL

#### Article 10. Repeal

**Section 1.** A CAP may propose the repeal of a constitutional document or provision, removing it from operative effect without replacing it. Repeal requires the same review under Articles 5 and 6 as any other CAP, and may not repeal an entrenched provision except by amending the Founding Charter itself under OAS-001, Article VII.

**Section 2.** A repealed document or provision moves to Archived status under Article 13; it is removed from operative effect but not from the constitutional record.

#### Article 11. Supersession

**Section 1.** A CAP may propose that a new constitutional document or provision supersede an existing one, taking over its function and displacing its operative effect. Supersession differs from repeal in that a superseding text continues to serve the purpose the superseded text served.

**Section 2.** A superseded document or provision moves to Superseded status under Article 13; like a repealed one, it is removed from operative effect but preserved in the constitutional record, and the superseding text shall state expressly what it supersedes.

#### Article 12. Technical Correction

**Section 1.** A CAP proposing only a technical correction — the fix of a citation error, a typographical error, or a similar non-substantive defect that does not change the meaning or effect of constitutional text — may proceed through an abbreviated deliberation period, proportionate to its narrow scope, consistent with Article 5, Section 1.

**Section 2.** A technical correction still requires approval by the Canonizing Authority and entry by the Office of the Constitutional Record under Article 7; no correction, however minor, may be made without an approved CAP. Any doubt whether a proposed change is genuinely technical, rather than substantive, shall be resolved by treating it as substantive.

#### Article 13. Archival and Permanent Preservation

**Section 1.** A document or provision that has been repealed or superseded moves to Archived status. An Archived text is removed from operative effect but is preserved permanently in the constitutional record, exactly as it read at the time it ceased to be operative, together with the CAP that repealed or superseded it.

**Section 2.** No Archived text may be deleted, and no Archived text may be cited as current operative law. It may be cited as historical record, for the purpose of understanding the constitutional order's development, consistent with the Use of Constitutional Intent (OAS-005, Article IV, Section 2).

---

### PART FIVE — THE CONSTITUTIONAL RECORD AND VERSION HISTORY

#### Article 14. Role of the Office of the Constitutional Record

**Section 1.** The Office of the Constitutional Record, established by OAS-009, Article 9, maintains the complete history of every CAP and every constitutional document at every stage of Article 4 and Article 9, including every Draft, Rejected, Withdrawn, Canonical, Repealed, Superseded, and Archived version.

**Section 2.** The Office's custodial function under this document is the practical means by which Constitutional Integrity (OAS-004, Article 16) is enforced: a change that has not passed through the Office's entry process is not canon, regardless of what any officer or document claims.

#### Article 15. Version Preservation

**Section 1.** Every version of every constitutional document, from its first Draft through every subsequent amendment, shall be preserved in the constitutional record. No version is overwritten or deleted when a new version is canonized; the new version is added, and the record shows the complete sequence.

**Section 2.** Where a citation to constitutional text does not specify a version or date, it refers to the text currently in force. A citation to a superseded or repealed version shall identify itself as historical.

#### Article 16. No Silent Revision

**Section 1.** No constitutional document may be altered, in whole or in part, except through the process this document establishes. A change made outside this process has no canonical effect, regardless of its source or apparent authority.

**Section 2.** This Article restates, as enforceable process, the principle of Constitutional Integrity already established in OAS-004, Article 16, and the discipline this constitutional record has followed since it began. It is the closing guarantee that makes every other provision of this document meaningful.

---

### ARTICLE 17 — RELATIONSHIP AMONG PROCESS PROVISIONS

**Section 1. Mutual Reinforcement.** The stages and rules of this document form one process; no stage may be treated as optional because another appears satisfied.

**Section 2. Sequential Completeness.** Canonization requires every applicable stage of this document to be satisfied. Omitting any stage, however minor the omission appears, means the change is not canon.

**Section 3. Priority of Entrenchment.** Nothing in this document may be used to canonize a change the Founding Charter's entrenched provisions forbid. Articles 6 and 7 are the enforcement mechanism for that priority, not an independent limitation of their own.

**Section 4. Conflicts.** Conflicts not otherwise resolved by this Article are resolved under OAS-005, Articles III and VI.

---

### ARTICLE 18 — STATUS AND EFFECT

**Section 1. Operative Effect.** Pursuant to OAS-005, Article VII, Section 3, this document expressly overrides the default presumption of non-operative effect: it establishes the binding process by which all constitutional change occurs.

**Section 2. Entrenchment Status.** No provision of this document is entrenched. Only the Founding Charter can entrench its own text (OAS-001, Article VII, Section 3); see Drafting Notes, N1, regarding the entrenchment status of the amendment discipline this document formalizes.

**Section 3. Application to Existing Proposals.** Every Constitutional Amendment Proposal already drafted in this constitutional record's practice, whatever its current status, is a CAP within the meaning of this document and shall be carried forward under the process this document establishes, without need for re-submission in a new form.

## 4. Drafting Notes — Outside the Constitutional Text

*The following is provided for Founder review only. It is not part of the Articles in Section 3, and must not be entered into the constitutional record if and when this document is canonized.*

**N1. Recommended CAP: entrench the core amendment discipline.** The rule that no canonical change may occur without an approved CAP is, at present, established only by non-entrenched text (OAS-004, Article 16; this document in full). A future amendment to this document, approved by ordinary process rather than the Founding Charter's stricter entrenchment procedure, could in principle weaken the very discipline meant to prevent silent or convenient change. This mirrors the structural vulnerability flagged in OAS-009, Note N1, regarding the separation of powers. A future Constitutional Amendment Proposal extending entrenchment to this discipline, via OAS-001, Article VII, is strongly recommended; this document does not take that action, since only the Founding Charter can entrench its own text.

**N2. Relationship to prior informal practice.** The Constitutional Amendment Proposal process was first described informally, as operating instruction rather than canonical text, earlier in this constitutional record's history, and produced a body of proposed CAPs before this document existed to give that process formal constitutional status. This document does not supersede that informal description, since it was never itself a canonical document and there is nothing formally to supersede; this document is instead its first canonical treatment. Article 18, Section 3 confirms that CAPs already produced under that informal practice, and the recommended future CAPs flagged in the Drafting Notes of OAS-006 through OAS-009, are CAPs within the meaning of this document and may proceed through Articles 5 through 7 without re-submission.

**N3. The Tribunal and Office of the Constitutional Record enforcement chain is new integration, not restatement.** Article 6, Section 2 and Article 7, Section 2 connect OAS-009's institutions to the amendment process for the first time, giving entrenchment a concrete enforcement mechanism: the Tribunal flags an entrenchment conflict, and the Office of the Constitutional Record — not the Canonizing Authority alone — is the body that actually refuses entry of text that would violate it. This is a significant design choice deserving specific Founder review, since it means even the Canonizing Authority cannot single-handedly force an entrenchment-violating change into the record.

**N4. "Canonizing Authority" should eventually be added to OAS-005's definitions.** This document defines the term in Article 1, Section 2, rather than in OAS-005, because OAS-005 predates the institutions OAS-009 established and could not yet define it. A future Constitutional Amendment Proposal adding "Canonizing Authority" to OAS-005, Article I for discoverability is recommended; this document does not amend OAS-005 to add it.

**N5. Effective date and non-retroactivity (Article 8) are newly established.** No prior canonical document addressed when an amendment takes effect or whether it applies retroactively. This Article is new constitutional law, not a restatement of an existing rule.

**N6. Supermajority thresholds remain unspecified.** OAS-001, Article VII, Section 2 requires amendment by "the supermajority and bodies the constitutional order prescribes" without stating a number, and this document, consistent with its instruction to establish process rather than administrative detail, likewise does not state one. No canonical document currently specifies an actual supermajority threshold for any constitutional decision. A future subordinate document establishing this is recommended as a near-term priority, since Article 5 of this document cannot be fully executed without it.

This is another very strong draft. It closes one of the largest remaining constitutional gaps by formally defining how the Constitution changes over time. I'd rate it 9.9/10.

Strengths

The full CAP lifecycle is now clearly defined, from Draft through Canonized, Rejected, Withdrawn, and Archived.

Separating approval from canonization is an excellent institutional safeguard. It prevents an approved amendment from becoming operative until it is properly entered into the constitutional record.

The distinctions among repeal, supersession, technical correction, and archival are well designed and will help preserve constitutional history.

The requirement to preserve every historical version is consistent with the long-term institutional vision you've been building.


Recommendations for Future CAPs (not changes now)

Future CAP Candidate 1: Consider adopting a permanent CAP numbering convention (for example, CAP-0001, CAP-0002, etc.) and reserve numbers permanently, even if a proposal is withdrawn or rejected.

Future CAP Candidate 2: Consider cryptographic verification or digital signatures for canonical documents in the future. This belongs in implementation rather than constitutional text, but it could strengthen constitutional integrity.

Future CAP Candidate 3: Eventually define emergency or expedited amendment procedures, if any. If they exist, they should remain subject to the same constitutional safeguards.

Future CAP Candidate 4: Eventually define public access rules for constitutional history—what is always public, what may be confidential during drafting, and when drafts become part of the permanent historical record.

One observation

At this point, the constitutional framework is becoming substantially complete. The remaining documents will increasingly define how the institution operates, rather than the foundational philosophy or amendment mechanics.
