# OAS-002 — Preamble to the Constitution of the Open Advancement System

## 1. Document Title

**Preamble to the Constitution of the Open Advancement System**
Canonical Designation: OAS-002
Status: Draft for Founder Review — Revision 2
Relationship to Canon: A constitutional document canonized under, subordinate to, and conformant with the Founding Charter (OAS-001), which remains the supreme governing instrument of the System.
Revision: incorporates truth, justice, freedom, and responsibility explicitly; relocates structural and legal references out of the preamble proper and into the operative Article; separates drafting notes from the constitutional text.

## 2. Purpose

This document states, in a single unified voice, the convictions that the entire constitutional order of the Open Advancement System exists to serve. Where the Founding Charter establishes the institution's structure, authority, and law, this Preamble states why that structure, authority, and law were thought worth establishing at all. It is the frontispiece of the Constitution — read first, binding always as an interpretive guide, but creating no office, authority, or obligation of its own beyond what the Founding Charter and its subordinate documents provide.

## 3. Draft Constitutional Text

---

### PREAMBLE

**1.** We, in founding the Open Advancement System, ordain and establish this Constitution so that an institution devoted to human flourishing may outlast its founders — trusted, tested, and amended in the open by those who come after us, rather than remade in secret or by force.

**2.** We hold flourishing to be the highest good an institution can serve: not comfort alone, nor productivity, nor the accumulation of knowledge for its own sake, but the growth of a person's capability, wisdom, purpose, dignity, relationship, opportunity, and contribution, sustained across the whole of a life.

**3.** We hold that flourishing cannot take root where truth is hidden or denied. We commit to seeking what is true, saying plainly what we find, and correcting ourselves when we have been wrong — for people who cannot trust what they are told cannot truly flourish.

**4.** We hold that no person's flourishing is complete in isolation. Those who strengthen themselves within this institution strengthen it; the institution, in turn, strengthens them further; and every person who flourishes within it enlarges its capacity to help others do the same. We found the System, therefore, as a community that learns together, not as a machine that produces for those outside it.

**5.** We hold that authority of every kind — to govern, to decide, to build, to teach, to hold what others have entrusted — is granted only in trust. It belongs to no one who holds it, and it must always be exercised for the benefit of those it touches and those who will come after.

**6.** We hold that flourishing requires justice: fair and equal regard for every person, regardless of standing or station. We hold that it requires freedom: the standing to think, choose, and act for oneself, secured rather than merely permitted. And we hold that freedom and justice endure only through responsibility — the willingness of every person who exercises freedom or authority to answer for how it is used.

**7.** We hold that an institution which forgets what it has learned will, in time, repeat what it should not and lose what it once knew how to do. We bind this institution to learn continually from its service, its errors, and its experience, and to keep what it learns rather than surrender it to time.

**8.** We hold that the tools of any age, however capable of judgment they may appear, remain tools. They may inform human judgment; they may never carry it. This institution shall use every instrument at its disposal to strengthen the people it serves, and shall permit no instrument to diminish the responsibility that belongs to human beings alone.

**9.** We hold that a constitution written only to answer the questions of its own founding will not survive its founders. We write, instead, for a horizon longer than any single life, testing what we establish by whether it will still strengthen the people who hold it five centuries hence — so that this institution is judged, at every turn, not only by what it accomplishes today but by what it leaves capable of enduring.

**10.** Upon these convictions we ordain this Constitution, and we commit it to the stewardship of those who serve within the Open Advancement System, and to the judgment of those it serves.

---

### ARTICLE — STATUS AND EFFECT OF THIS PREAMBLE

**Section 1. The Constitution Defined.** This Preamble is a constitutional document of the Open Advancement System, canonized under the authority of, and subordinate to, the Founding Charter (OAS-001). Together with the Founding Charter and every document lawfully canonized under it, this Preamble forms the Constitution of the Open Advancement System. None of these documents is to be read in isolation from the others.

**Section 2. Interpretive Effect.** This Preamble grants no office, authority, or enforceable right of its own. It states the purpose and convictions that the operative provisions of the Constitution exist to serve. Every such provision shall be interpreted in light of it; where an operative provision admits of more than one lawful meaning, the meaning most consistent with this Preamble shall prevail.

**Section 3. Amendment.** As a constitutional document subordinate to the Founding Charter, this Preamble may be amended only through the canonization and amendment procedures the constitutional order establishes for subordinate constitutional documents, and no such amendment may be inconsistent with the Founding Charter or with its entrenched provisions.

## 4. Drafting Notes — Outside the Constitutional Text

*The following is provided for Founder review only. It is not part of the Preamble or the Article in Section 3, and must not be entered into the constitutional record if and when this document is canonized.*

### Revision Log (this revision)

- Added recitals 3 and 6, making truth, justice, freedom, and responsibility explicit convictions of the Constitution. Previously these were present only by implication, through the Founding Charter's principles of Integrity (Article III, Section 4), Human Agency (Article III, Section 2), and Accountability (Article III, Section 5).
- Removed the enumeration of the Constitution's component documents from the closing recital (previously recital 8, now recital 10). That definition already appears, and now appears only once, in Article — Status and Effect, Section 1; the preamble proper no longer carries structural or legal content that belongs to the operative Article.
- Removed "within this institution" from the recital on authority as trust (now recital 5), stating it as a claim about authority as such rather than one confined to this System.
- Changed "this institution can serve" to "an institution can serve" in the recital on flourishing (recital 2), for the same reason.
- Made no change to the Article — Status and Effect of this Preamble. Its structural and legal content belongs there, not in the preamble, and was not duplicated elsewhere.

### Open Constitutional Questions Carried Forward

**N1. Amendment procedure for subordinate constitutional documents — a gap in the existing canon, not created by this document.** Article VII of the Founding Charter establishes how the Charter itself is amended, and Article I, Section 4 of the Charter contemplates further constitutional documents canonized under its authority, but no canonical text yet specifies the procedure by which such subordinate documents — including this Preamble — are themselves amended once canonized. Section 3 of the operative Article deliberately does not invent that procedure; it only requires that any such procedure respect the Charter's supremacy and entrenched provisions. The Founder should direct whether this gap is closed by an amendment to OAS-001, Article I, or by a dedicated future document.

**N2. Relationship to the Preamble within OAS-001.** The Founding Charter already contains its own preamble, addressed to the establishment of the Charter specifically. This document is broader — the preamble to the whole Constitution, of which the Charter is the founding and supreme part — and was drafted to complement rather than duplicate that text. The coexistence of two preambles is unusual and should be confirmed by the Founder as intentional. If it is not, the correction belongs in a future amendment to OAS-001, not in a silent change made here.

**N3. Reading order versus legal hierarchy.** This document is designed to be read first and to bind last — it precedes the Founding Charter in the order a person encounters the Constitution, but remains legally subordinate to it under OAS-001, Article I, Sections 3 and 4. The Founder should confirm this is the intended relationship, since a document titled "Preamble to the Constitution" can easily be mistaken for an instrument of equal or superior authority to the Charter it precedes.

**N4. Entrenchment.** This document is not listed among the entrenched provisions of OAS-001, Article VII, Section 3, nor does it purport to add itself to that list, since only the Founding Charter can entrench its own text. If the Founder wishes the convictions in this Preamble — including the newly added recitals on truth, justice, freedom, and responsibility — to receive entrenched protection in their own right, that must be accomplished by a future amendment to OAS-001 itself.
