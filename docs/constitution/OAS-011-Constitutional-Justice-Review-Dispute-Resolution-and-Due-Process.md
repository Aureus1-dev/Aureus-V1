# OAS-011 — Constitutional Justice, Review, Dispute Resolution, and Due Process

## 1. Document Title

**Constitutional Justice, Review, Dispute Resolution, and Due Process**
Canonical Designation: OAS-011
Status: Draft for Founder Review
Relationship to Canon: A constitutional document canonized under, subordinate to, and conformant with the Founding Charter (OAS-001), and consistent with OAS-002 through OAS-010. Governed by the default rules of OAS-005, Article VII, except where this document expressly provides otherwise.

## 2. Purpose

This document establishes the justice system of the Open Advancement System: the due process every person is owed, the jurisdiction and independence of the Tribunal established in OAS-009, the standards by which disputes are heard and decided, the remedies available when this Constitution is violated, and the appeal and finality of Tribunal decisions. It gives the Tribunal's authority, stated in outline in OAS-009, Article 6, its full operative jurisdiction and procedure, and closes the gap — flagged in the Drafting Notes of OAS-007 and OAS-008 — that left disputes between persons themselves without any forum. It does not amend OAS-006, OAS-007, or OAS-009; it completes what each already anticipated.

## 3. Draft Constitutional Text

---

### PART ONE — CONSTITUTIONAL DUE PROCESS

#### Article 1. Due Process Defined

**Section 1.** Constitutional due process is the combined guarantee, already established across OAS-006, that no person is subject to a decision of the System's authority without Notice, Explanation, an opportunity for Human Review, and access to Petition and Redress (OAS-006, Articles 6 through 9). This Article names that combined guarantee for the first time; it does not add to or narrow the rights it names.

**Section 2.** Due process applies, without distinction, to Guests, Participants, Contributors, Members, Stewards, and organizations, to the extent OAS-006 and OAS-008 extend the underlying rights to each. This document supplies the forum in which due process is exercised; it does not alter who holds it.

#### Article 2. Protection Against Arbitrary Action

**Section 1.** No act of the System's authority affecting a person may rest on reasons the System is unwilling or unable to state. This Article gives adjudicative form to the Presumption Against Instrumentalization (OAS-006, Article 13) and to Justice (OAS-004, Article 3).

**Section 2.** A decision defended, when challenged, only by reference to the deciding officer's discretion, without a reason a reviewing body can examine, does not satisfy this Article and is subject to correction under Part Five.

---

### PART TWO — JURISDICTION OF THE TRIBUNAL

#### Article 3. General Jurisdiction

**Section 1.** The Tribunal holds jurisdiction over the conduct of every officer, body, and instrument of the System, including the Council and the Administration, as OAS-009, Article 6, Section 1 already establishes. This Article restates that jurisdiction and does not amend it.

**Section 2.** The Tribunal's jurisdiction under this Part is exercised through the process this document establishes, in the same manner OAS-010 establishes the process through which the Tribunal exercises its role in reviewing Constitutional Amendment Proposals.

#### Article 4. Adjudicative Jurisdiction Distinguished from Amendment Review

**Section 1.** The Tribunal exercises two distinct functions under this Constitution: review of Constitutional Amendment Proposals for conflict with entrenched provisions, as OAS-010, Article 6 establishes, and adjudication of disputes and petitions under this document. These functions are related but not identical.

**Section 2.** A Tribunal decision under this document resolving a specific dispute does not itself amend the Constitution, even where it identifies an ambiguity or a provision in need of revision. Any such finding proceeds, as OAS-009, Article 6, Section 2 already provides, as a recommendation to the Council through the Constitutional Amendment Proposal process.

#### Article 5. Jurisdiction Over Disputes Between Persons

**Section 1.** The Tribunal holds jurisdiction to resolve a dispute between two or more persons arising from an alleged breach of a duty owed between them under OAS-007 — including the Duty of Mutual Dignity (OAS-007, Article 1), the Duty to Respect the Agency of Others (Article 2), the Duty of Fair Dealing (Article 3), and the Duty to Respect the Privacy of Others (Article 5). This Article is established here for the first time and closes the gap identified in the Drafting Notes of OAS-007 (N5) and OAS-008 (N4).

**Section 2.** This jurisdiction extends to disputes between Members, between a Member and a Guest or Participant, and between any persons whose relationship to the System brings the dispute within this Constitution's scope. It does not extend to a dispute wholly unconnected to a person's engagement with the System, which remains outside this Constitution's reach.

#### Article 6. Jurisdiction Over Organizations

**Section 1.** The Tribunal holds the same jurisdiction over a dispute involving an organization, as Guest, Participant, Contributor, or otherwise, that it holds over a dispute involving a natural person, to the extent OAS-006, Article 17 and OAS-007, Article 18 extend the underlying rights and duties to organizations.

**Section 2.** This Article does not resolve the broader gap, already flagged in OAS-006, OAS-007, and OAS-008, that "organization" remains undefined as a foundational category; it applies existing, provisional treatment to adjudication and awaits the same future document those prior notes anticipate.

---

### PART THREE — INDEPENDENCE AND IMPARTIALITY

#### Article 7. Independence of Review

**Section 1.** A Tribunal Steward's decision in a specific matter may not be directed, overridden, or reversed by the Council, the Administration, or any other body, except through the appeal process this document establishes, or the Constitutional Amendment Proposal process where a decision reveals a need for constitutional change.

**Section 2.** This Article implements, for the Tribunal specifically, the separation of powers already required by OAS-001, Article V, Section 2, and the comity requirements of OAS-009, Article 10.

#### Article 8. Impartiality and Recusal

**Section 1.** A Tribunal Steward who holds a personal interest in a matter, or who is a party to the dispute, may not participate in deciding it. This is a new procedural safeguard, established here for the first time, grounded in the Right to Fair Treatment (OAS-006, Article 3) and the Duty of Fair Dealing (OAS-007, Article 3).

**Section 2.** Where recusal would leave the Tribunal without a quorum to decide a matter, the continuity provisions of OAS-009, Article 14 govern; this Article does not itself resolve that circumstance.

---

### PART FOUR — PROCESS OF CONSTITUTIONAL REVIEW

#### Article 9. Initiating Review

**Section 1.** A matter comes before the Tribunal through a Petition under OAS-006, Article 9, a referral from the Council or the Administration, or a complaint by one person against another under Article 5 of this document.

**Section 2.** The Tribunal shall provide the person or persons against whom a matter is brought the Notice and Explanation already guaranteed by OAS-006, Articles 6 and 7, before deciding it.

#### Article 10. Standards of Evidence and Fairness

**Section 1.** Every party to a matter before the Tribunal holds the right to be heard, to know the basis of any claim against them, and to respond to it, before a decision is made. The Tribunal's decision shall be based on the record before it and shall state the reasoning that supports it, consistent with the Right to Explanation (OAS-006, Article 7) and the Duty of Honesty (OAS-007, Article 4).

**Section 2.** This Article establishes the constitutional floor of fairness every Tribunal proceeding must meet. It does not establish specific evidentiary rules, burdens of proof, or procedural mechanics, which remain reserved to subordinate governance documents consistent with this floor.

#### Article 11. Constitutional Interpretation During Adjudication

**Section 1.** The Tribunal shall interpret constitutional text according to OAS-005, Articles IV and V, and shall resolve genuine ambiguity according to OAS-005, Article VI, recording the ambiguity and the reading chosen as that Article requires.

**Section 2.** The Tribunal's interpretation in a specific matter binds the parties to that matter. It does not bind the Tribunal's own future decisions, which remain free to interpret the same provision differently upon fuller consideration, though a departure from a prior interpretation should be stated openly and explained, consistent with Truth (OAS-004, Article 5) and Constitutional Integrity (OAS-004, Article 16).

---

### PART FIVE — REMEDIES

#### Article 12. Available Remedies

**Section 1.** Upon finding a violation of this Constitution, the Tribunal may: declare the violation and its nature; order the reversal or correction of the act found to violate this Constitution; order the restoration of a right, status, or standing wrongfully denied, including membership under OAS-008, Article 20; and refer the matter to the Council as a recommendation for a Constitutional Amendment Proposal where the violation reveals a defect in constitutional text itself.

**Section 2.** This Article establishes the categories of remedy available at the constitutional level. It does not establish schedules of damages, compensation formulas, or other operational remedies, which remain reserved to subordinate governance documents, consistent with those documents' obligation not to fall below the floor this Constitution establishes.

#### Article 13. Proportionality of Remedies

**Section 1.** A remedy ordered by the Tribunal shall be proportionate to the violation found, and shall go no further than necessary to correct the violation and restore the person affected to the position they would have held absent it.

**Section 2.** This Article does not limit the Tribunal's authority to order a full remedy for a serious violation; it forbids only a remedy disproportionate to what occurred, consistent with the Presumption Against Instrumentalization (OAS-006, Article 13) applied to the System's own corrective action.

---

### PART SIX — APPEALS AND FINALITY

#### Article 14. Appeal Within the Tribunal

**Section 1.** A person aggrieved by a Tribunal decision holds the right to have that decision reviewed by the full Tribunal or a larger panel of it, as subordinate governance documents establish, provided the reviewing body was not the same body that made the original decision.

**Section 2.** This Article establishes the right to at least one layer of internal review beyond an initial decision. It does not establish a specific number of appellate layers or their administration, which remain reserved to subordinate governance documents.

#### Article 15. Finality

**Section 1.** A Tribunal decision becomes final when the appeal right of Article 14 has been exercised or has lapsed unexercised. A final decision resolves the specific matter it decided and may not be reopened by the same parties on the same facts, except as Section 2 provides.

**Section 2.** A final decision may be reopened where new evidence emerges that could not reasonably have been available at the time of the original decision, or where the constitutional text on which it rested is later amended in a manner that would have changed the outcome, consistent with the non-retroactivity default of OAS-010, Article 8.

---

### PART SEVEN — JUSTICE, RIGHTS, DUTIES, GOVERNANCE, AND CONSTITUTIONAL INTEGRITY

#### Article 16. The Relationship Among Them

**Section 1.** Justice, as this document gives it institutional form, is the means by which the rights of OAS-006 are made real, the duties of OAS-007 are fairly assessed, the governance structures of OAS-009 remain accountable to the Constitution rather than to themselves, and Constitutional Integrity (OAS-004, Article 16) is enforced against every officer, body, and instrument without exception.

**Section 2.** A constitutional order that declares rights and duties but supplies no means of adjudicating them has declared aspirations, not law. This document exists so that every right, every duty, and every limit this Constitution establishes has a forum in which it can be tested and enforced.

---

### ARTICLE 17 — RELATIONSHIP AMONG PROVISIONS OF THIS DOCUMENT

**Section 1. Mutual Reinforcement.** The Parts of this document are not independent grants to be read in isolation; they are one system of constitutional justice and are to be read together.

**Section 2. No Hierarchy Among Parts.** No Part of this document is superior to another. The order of Parts is expository, not a hierarchy of constitutional weight.

**Section 3. Priority of Rights.** Nothing in this document diminishes any right established in OAS-006 or alters any duty established in OAS-007. Every institution and process this document establishes is bound by both in full.

**Section 4. Conflicts.** Conflicts not otherwise resolved by this Article are resolved under OAS-005, Articles III and VI.

---

### ARTICLE 18 — STATUS AND EFFECT

**Section 1. Operative Effect.** Pursuant to OAS-005, Article VII, Section 3, this document expressly overrides the default presumption of non-operative effect: it establishes the binding jurisdiction, procedure, and remedial authority of the Tribunal described in Parts One through Six.

**Section 2. Entrenchment Status.** No provision of this document is entrenched. Only the Founding Charter can entrench its own text (OAS-001, Article VII, Section 3); see Drafting Notes, N1, regarding Tribunal independence specifically.

**Section 3. Relationship to OAS-009.** This document completes, rather than amends, the Tribunal's jurisdiction as OAS-009, Article 6 establishes it in outline. In every case of apparent tension between this document and OAS-009, this document's Part Two governs the Tribunal's adjudicative jurisdiction specifically, and OAS-009 governs the Tribunal's institutional composition and standing generally.

---

## 4. Drafting Notes — Outside the Constitutional Text

*The following is provided for Founder review only. It is not part of the Articles in Section 3, and must not be entered into the constitutional record if and when this document is canonized.*

**N1. Recommended CAP: entrench Tribunal independence.** Article 7 is, structurally, among the most important protections in this document: a Tribunal that can be overridden case by case is not a genuine check on the Council or the Administration. It rests, at present, on non-entrenched text, mirroring the same vulnerability flagged in OAS-009 (N1) and OAS-010 (N1) regarding the separation of powers and the amendment discipline generally. A future Constitutional Amendment Proposal extending entrenchment to Article 7, or to OAS-001, Article V, Section 2 generally, is recommended; this document does not take that action.

**N2. Article 5 is a significant, deliberate expansion of institutional authority.** Extending Tribunal jurisdiction to disputes between persons — not only disputes between a person and the System — closes the gaps flagged in OAS-007 (N5) and OAS-008 (N4), but it is new law, not restatement, and gives the Tribunal real power over private disputes between individuals. The Founder should specifically confirm this scope was intended, including the limiting principle in Article 5, Section 2 that confines jurisdiction to disputes connected to a person's engagement with the System.

**N3. The non-binding-precedent design (Article 11, Section 2) was a considered choice.** An alternative model, in which a Tribunal decision binds future decisions on similar facts, was considered and rejected, to preserve the Constitutional Amendment Proposal process as the sole route to genuine constitutional change and to prevent adjudication from becoming a means of amending the Constitution without going through OAS-010. Reasonable constitutional systems differ on this question, and the Founder should confirm this choice.

**N4. Remedies are stated only as categories.** Article 12 does not establish compensation schedules, restitution formulas, or similar operational detail, consistent with this document's constitutional scope. This leaves the Tribunal with broad discretion in fashioning specific remedies within the stated categories. A future subordinate document narrowing this discretion, once the System has operating experience to draw on, is recommended but not undertaken here.

**N5. Appeal of Tribunal decisions and removal of Tribunal members are different matters.** Article 14 of this document governs appeal of a Tribunal decision on the merits of a dispute. OAS-009, Article 8, Section 2 governs removal of a Tribunal Steward from office, reviewed by the Council. This document does not address, and should not be read to resolve, the question OAS-009's Drafting Notes (N4) already flagged regarding the design of that removal-review process.

**N6. This document does not address disputes between constitutional bodies.** A jurisdictional dispute between the Council and the Administration, or any other conflict between constitutional bodies as institutions, is already governed by OAS-009, Article 11, resolved under OAS-005, Article III. This document addresses disputes involving persons, Members, Stewards, and organizations; it does not duplicate or extend OAS-009's separate treatment of inter-body disputes.

Overall Assessment

OAS-011 successfully completes a major structural gap that earlier documents intentionally left open. OAS-006 established rights, OAS-007 established duties, OAS-009 established the Tribunal, and OAS-011 now gives the Tribunal meaningful constitutional jurisdiction and process.

The document remains disciplined by avoiding operational details while providing a strong constitutional framework.

No immediate constitutional revisions are necessary before continuing.


---

Future CAP Candidate 1 — Constitutional Independence of Justice

Article 7 protects Tribunal independence, but future constitutional growth may justify recognizing Judicial Independence as its own constitutional principle within OAS-004 or by entrenching portions of OAS-011.

For now, leaving it as a recommendation is appropriate.


---

Future CAP Candidate 2 — Constitutional Principles of Restoration

Current remedies focus primarily on correcting constitutional violations.

In future iterations, consider expressly recognizing:

Restoration

Reconciliation

Rehabilitation

Prevention of repeated harm


These would reinforce the institution's stewardship philosophy without replacing accountability.


---

Future CAP Candidate 3 — Constitutional Mediation

Currently every dispute ultimately proceeds toward adjudication.

Future constitutional development may distinguish:

mediation

facilitated reconciliation

arbitration

constitutional adjudication


This would encourage resolution before formal constitutional judgment while preserving Tribunal authority.


---

Future CAP Candidate 4 — Constitutional Access to Justice

Eventually consider recognizing a constitutional principle that justice should remain reasonably accessible regardless of wealth, position, education, or institutional familiarity.

This would complement Human Dignity, Human Agency, and Justice without changing any current text.


---

Future CAP Candidate 5 — Institutional Learning From Cases

The Tribunal will gradually accumulate constitutional experience.

Eventually consider a mechanism requiring anonymous lessons learned from constitutional cases to become part of Institutional Learning while protecting individual privacy.

This fits naturally with OAS-004's Institutional Learning principle.


---

Future CAP Candidate 6 — Constitutional Performance Review

Several decades from now, the Constitution may benefit from a recurring constitutional audit asking questions such as:

Are rights actually protecting people?

Are duties producing flourishing?

Is justice functioning fairly?

Are governance structures still serving the Mission?

What has experience taught us?


This would institutionalize constitutional self-improvement while remaining consistent with Continuous Improvement.
