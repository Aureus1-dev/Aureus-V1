# OAS-001 — Founding Charter of the Open Advancement System

## 1. Document Title

**Founding Charter of the Open Advancement System (OAS)**
Canonical Designation: OAS-001
Status: **Draft 0.95** — Constitutional Revision of Draft 0.9, integrating canonical institutional philosophy. Not yet ratified.

## 2. Purpose

This Charter establishes the Open Advancement System as an enduring institution. It declares the purpose for which the institution exists, entrenches the principles by which it must act, defines the relationship between human authority and technological capability, establishes the supremacy and hierarchy of the constitutional order, and provides for its own amendment and continuity. All other constitutional documents, policies, and instruments of the Open Advancement System derive their authority from, and must conform to, this Charter.

## 3. Draft Constitutional Text

---

### PREAMBLE

We establish the Open Advancement System in the conviction that every person is capable of flourishing — of growing in capability, wisdom, purpose, dignity, relationship, and contribution across a lifetime — and that this flourishing is worth strengthening, in each person and across generations. We hold that the means of advancement should be open to those we serve; that those who strengthen themselves increase the capacity of all to help one another; and that the tools of any age, however powerful, must remain servants of human purpose.

We found this institution to endure. We therefore bind it not to the technologies, personalities, or circumstances of its founding, but to principles capable of governing conduct we cannot foresee, and capable of still strengthening humanity five centuries hence. We entrust its stewardship to the people who will serve within it — not as owners, but as trustees for those they serve and those who will come after — and we hold them, and all who follow them, accountable to this Charter.

---

### ARTICLE I — ESTABLISHMENT

**Section 1. Name.** The institution established by this Charter is the Open Advancement System, herein "the System" or "OAS."

**Section 2. Perpetual Character.** The System is established with the intention of perpetual existence. No provision of this Charter, and no act of any officer or body of the System, shall be construed to favor short-term advantage over the long-term survival and integrity of the mission, except as this Charter expressly provides for dissolution.

**Section 3. Supremacy of the Charter.** This Charter is the supreme governing instrument of the System. Every constitutional document, bylaw, policy, procedure, contract, and decision of the System must conform to it. Any instrument or act inconsistent with this Charter is void to the extent of the inconsistency.

**Section 4. Constitutional Order.** The constitutional order of the System consists of this Charter and such further constitutional documents as are canonized under the authority of this Charter. Where constitutional documents conflict, this Charter prevails; among subordinate constitutional documents, the conflict shall be resolved by the amendment process rather than by silent reinterpretation.

---

### ARTICLE II — MISSION

**Section 1. Mission.** The mission of the Open Advancement System is to help every person flourish. The System pursues this mission by strengthening human capability, knowledge, and agency, and by making the means of advancement openly available to those it serves, across generations.

**Section 2. Human Development.** Flourishing, as this Charter uses the term, is not reducible to productivity or information. It consists in the growth of capability, wisdom, purpose, dignity, relationship, opportunity, and meaningful contribution. The System shall pursue all of these together, and shall not mistake advancement in any one of them for the whole.

**Section 3. The Lifelong Journey.** Every person's flourishing is a lifelong journey of growth, not a fixed state to be achieved and left behind. The System shall serve people at every stage of that journey, helping each discover and take the next meaningful step available to them, rather than prescribing a single path all must follow.

**Section 4. The Collective.** The System is a collective learning community. Its members strengthen the institution, and the institution strengthens its members; the flourishing of each member increases the System's capacity to help others flourish. The System shall treat the flourishing of those within it as inseparable from, and never as competing with, the flourishing of those it serves.

**Section 5. Primacy of the Mission.** The mission is the reason the System exists. The institution, its assets, its offices, and its technologies exist to serve the mission. The mission shall never be subordinated to the preservation of the institution, the convenience of its officers, the demands of its tools, or the interests of any external party.

**Section 6. Interpretation.** Every provision of this Charter, and every act taken under it, shall be interpreted in the manner that best serves the mission while respecting the principles entrenched in Article III. Where two lawful interpretations are available, the interpretation that better protects human dignity and agency shall prevail. Where the long-term effect of an interpretation is uncertain, it shall be tested by whether it would still strengthen humanity five centuries hence; an interpretation that would not withstand that test shall yield to one that would.

---

### ARTICLE III — FOUNDING PRINCIPLES

The following principles bind every officer, body, and instrument of the System. They govern the exercise of all authority created by this Charter and by any subordinate document.

**Section 1. Human Dignity.** The System shall treat every person it serves, employs, or affects as an end in themselves. No purpose of the System justifies degrading, deceiving, or instrumentalizing a human being.

**Section 2. Human Agency.** The System exists to enlarge, not to replace, the capacity of people to understand, decide, and act for themselves. The System shall not cultivate dependence where it can cultivate capability.

**Section 3. Openness.** The System shall make its knowledge, methods, and benefits as openly available as is consistent with the safety of persons, the rights of others, and the integrity of the mission. Secrecy within the System is the exception, must be justified, and must be bounded in scope and time.

**Section 4. Integrity.** The System shall not misrepresent itself, its work, or its results. It shall acknowledge error, correct the record, and bear the cost of honesty rather than pass that cost to those it serves.

**Section 5. Accountability.** Every grant of authority within the System carries a corresponding duty to answer for its exercise. No office, body, or instrument of the System shall hold authority without an identified means by which that authority can be reviewed, checked, or withdrawn.

**Section 6. Stewardship.** Every authority within the System is held in trust — for those upon whom it is exercised, for those the System serves, and for those who will inherit the System. No officer, body, or instrument owns the authority entrusted to it; each holds it only in trust, and must exercise it for the benefit of those it serves rather than for its own advantage. Those who govern the System shall not consume, encumber, or endanger its capacity to pursue the mission in the future for advantage in the present.

**Section 7. Anticipation of Abuse.** The System shall be governed on the assumption that any power it creates may one day be sought by those who would misuse it. Its structures shall be designed so that the misuse of any single office, body, or instrument can be detected, resisted, and corrected.

**Section 8. Institutional Learning.** The System shall continually learn from its service, its experience, its evidence, and its own reflection. No lesson that would strengthen the mission shall be lost through neglect, turnover, or the passage of time. The System shall maintain the means to preserve what it learns and to apply that learning to its future conduct.

---

### ARTICLE IV — HUMAN–AI STEWARDSHIP

**Section 1. AI as Stewardship Technology.** Artificial intelligence and every other technology employed by the System are stewardship technologies: they exist to strengthen human judgment, human capability, and human flourishing. Technology serves the mission; the mission never serves technology.

**Section 2. Human Accountability.** Every institutional decision of the System is a human decision. Where artificial intelligence or any automated instrument informs, prepares, or executes an act of the System, an identified human officer or body remains fully accountable for that act. No officer may disclaim responsibility on the ground that a decision was produced or recommended by a machine. An officer may use artificial intelligence to inform a decision; no officer may use it to escape responsibility for one.

**Section 3. Reserved Human Judgment.** No instrument, however capable, shall hold constitutional office, exercise constitutional authority, cast a vote in any governing body, or serve as the final authority over any decision materially affecting a person's rights, standing, or dignity within the System. Such decisions are reserved to accountable human judgment.

**Section 4. Intelligibility and Contestability.** Where automated instruments materially affect a person, that person is entitled to know that such an instrument was involved, to receive an intelligible account of the basis of the decision, and to have the decision reviewed by an accountable human being.

**Section 5. Technological Neutrality of the Charter.** This Article binds the System with respect to all present and future technologies of comparable function, whatever their form or name. Changes in technology do not diminish the obligations of this Article; they extend them.

---

### ARTICLE V — GOVERNANCE

**Section 1. Government Under the Charter.** All governance of the System is exercised under this Charter and is limited by it. The specific composition, selection, tenure, and procedures of the System's governing bodies and offices shall be established by subordinate constitutional documents canonized under this Charter, and shall at all times satisfy the requirements of this Article.

**Section 2. Separation of Powers.** The constitutional order shall separate, at minimum: the authority to set direction and canon; the authority to execute and administer; and the authority to review conduct against this Charter. No single person shall permanently hold all three authorities.

**Section 3. Limits on Office.** Every office of the System shall be created by written constitutional or subordinate instrument that defines its authority, its duties, its accountability, and the means by which its holder may be removed. Authority not granted in writing is not held.

**Section 4. The Founder.** During the founding period, the Founder holds the authority to approve and canonize constitutional documents and to resolve constitutional questions this Charter reserves to Founder approval. The constitutional order shall provide for the orderly transfer of the Founder's constitutional authorities to durable institutional bodies, so that the System does not depend upon any single person for its continuity.

**Section 5. Duty of Officers.** Every officer of the System owes their loyalty, within the System, to the mission and to this Charter — not to any superior, faction, benefactor, or instrument. An order inconsistent with this Charter is without authority, and no officer is obliged to execute it.

---

### ARTICLE VI — ACCOUNTABILITY AND THE RECORD

**Section 1. The Constitutional Record.** The System shall maintain a permanent, protected record of its constitutional documents, canonical decisions, and amendments. The record shall state what was decided, by whom, under what authority, and when. No canonical decision is effective until entered in the record.

**Section 2. Transparency of Governance.** The governance of the System shall be conducted so that those the System serves can know how authority is held and how it has been used. Withholding of governance information must be justified under Article III, Section 3, and recorded as an exception.

**Section 3. Right of Petition and Redress.** Any person materially affected by an act of the System may petition for review of that act. The constitutional order shall provide accessible means of review and genuine remedies, administered by accountable human beings.

**Section 4. Duty to Report Violation.** Every officer who becomes aware of a violation of this Charter has a duty to report it through the means the constitutional order provides. The System shall protect any person from retaliation for a good-faith report.

**Section 5. Institutional Memory.** Beyond its constitutional record, the System shall preserve the lessons drawn from its service, its errors, and its experience, and shall make them available to those who govern and serve within it. A lesson once learned and then lost through neglect is a failure of stewardship.

---

### ARTICLE VII — AMENDMENT

**Section 1. Amendability.** This Charter may be amended only by the deliberate process this Article establishes. No amendment is effective by practice, silence, interpretation, or emergency.

**Section 2. Process.** An amendment must be proposed in writing, published within the System for a period of deliberation, adopted by the supermajority and bodies the constitutional order prescribes, and entered in the constitutional record. During the founding period, amendment additionally requires the approval of the Founder.

**Section 3. Entrenched Provisions.** The following may not be amended in a manner that weakens them: the mission of human flourishing (Article II, Section 1); the primacy of the mission (Article II, Section 5); the principles of human dignity and human agency (Article III, Sections 1 and 2); the principle of stewardship (Article III, Section 6); human accountability for institutional decisions (Article IV, Sections 2 and 3); the requirement that authority be matched by accountability (Article III, Section 5); and this Section. An amendment purporting to weaken an entrenched provision is void.

**Section 4. Conforming Documents.** Upon amendment of this Charter, all subordinate documents inconsistent with the amendment are void to the extent of the inconsistency, and the responsible bodies shall conform them without delay.

---

### ARTICLE VIII — CONTINUITY AND DISSOLUTION

**Section 1. Continuity.** The System shall maintain, in its constitutional order, provision for succession in every constitutional office and for the continuity of governance through crisis, so that no vacancy, disaster, or failure of technology suspends the authority of this Charter.

**Section 2. Emergency Powers.** Any emergency authority created under this Charter must be limited in scope, limited in duration, subject to review, and incapable of amending the constitutional order. This Charter remains in full force in every emergency.

**Section 3. Dissolution.** The System may be dissolved only upon a determination, made by the deliberate supermajority process the constitutional order prescribes, that the mission can no longer be served by the System's continued existence. Upon dissolution, the System's assets and knowledge shall be applied, to the fullest extent lawful, to purposes consistent with the mission and the principles of Article III, and its constitutional record shall be preserved.

---

### ARTICLE IX — RATIFICATION

This Charter takes effect upon its approval and canonization by the Founder and its entry into the constitutional record, and thereupon binds the Open Advancement System, its officers, its bodies, and its instruments, in perpetuity, subject only to amendment under Article VII.

## 4. Notes — Constitutional Questions Requiring Founder Approval

The following updates and clarifies the notes carried over from Draft 0.9 in light of this revision.

**N1. Canonical mission statement — narrowed.** Draft 0.9 flagged the mission's wording as open. This revision resolves the core question: flourishing is now stated as the end, with capability, knowledge, and agency as means (Article II, Section 1). What remains open is only whether "openly available" should be read as a default of open publication or of open access to benefit; Founder direction is still needed on that narrower point.

**N2. Definition of "members" and "those the System serves."** The Collective (Article II, Section 4) now makes the institution's character as a learning community constitutionally binding, but still does not define membership. This question is now more urgent than in Draft 0.9, since Section 4 creates rights and duties that presuppose a knowable class of "members" distinct from — but not opposed to — "those the System serves." The Founder should direct whether a Charter of Membership is required as a companion document.

**N3. Governance architecture (Article V).** Unchanged from Draft 0.9. Still deferred to subordinate documents by design.

**N4. Founding period and Founder authority.** Unchanged and still the most urgent open structural question. The addition of Article III, Section 6 (Stewardship, broadened to bind every authority in the System, not only future-facing governance) makes the Founder's own authority explicitly a stewardship, not an ownership — which strengthens, but does not resolve, the need for a defined sunset mechanism.

**N5. Amendment thresholds.** Unchanged from Draft 0.9.

**N6. Entrenchment scope — revised, requires fresh confirmation.** Article VII, Section 3 has been updated to add the mission of human flourishing and the broadened stewardship principle, and to correct cross-references following the renumbering of Article II. Human Development (Article II, Section 2), the Lifelong Journey (Article II, Section 3), the Collective (Article II, Section 4), and Institutional Learning (Article III, Section 8) were deliberately left unentrenched: they are fully binding constitutional text, but the drafting judgment was that entrenching every new principle risks ossifying language that later generations may need to refine without threatening the mission itself. The Founder should confirm this judgment or direct otherwise.

**N7. Relationship to external law.** Unchanged from Draft 0.9.

**N8. Dissolution destination.** Unchanged from Draft 0.9; unaffected by this revision since Article VIII, Section 3 already cross-refers to "the mission," which now carries the flourishing framing automatically.

**N9 (new). Institutional Learning and the Record.** Article III, Section 8 establishes learning as a founding principle; Article VI, Section 5 gives it a constitutional anchor in the record-keeping article. Neither section specifies a mechanism, by design — that belongs in a subordinate document. The Founder should direct whether a dedicated instrument (e.g., a Charter of Institutional Memory) is required, or whether this is left to ordinary policy.

## 5. Changelog — Draft 0.9 → Draft 0.95

Each entry states what changed, where, and why it strengthens the Charter rather than merely elaborating it.

1. **Preamble — rewritten to lead with flourishing and trusteeship.**
   *Why:* Draft 0.9 opened with capability, judgment, and dignity as freestanding goods. Canonical principle 1 requires flourishing to be the ultimate purpose, with the rest as its content. The rewritten Preamble also introduces stewardship-as-trusteeship ("not as owners, but as trustees") and previews the Collective ("increase the capacity of all to help one another") without pre-empting their fuller constitutional statement later in the document — consistent with a preamble's role of stating conviction, not law.

2. **Article II, Section 1 (Mission) — reframed around flourishing as the end.**
   *Why:* Implements canonical principle 1 directly. Capability, knowledge, and agency are retained but repositioned as the means by which the System pursues flourishing, not as co-equal ends. This is the single most consequential change in the revision, since Article II is the interpretive anchor for the entire Charter (Article II, Section 6).

3. **Article II, Section 2 (Human Development) — new.**
   *Why:* Implements canonical principle 5. Without this section, "flourishing" would be an undefined term of art, vulnerable to narrowing by future officers into mere productivity or information metrics — precisely the failure mode principle 5 was adopted to prevent. Stating the components of flourishing at the constitutional level, without prescribing how to measure or pursue them, keeps the provision durable rather than administrative.

4. **Article II, Section 3 (The Lifelong Journey) — new.**
   *Why:* Implements canonical principle 7. Deliberately avoids the word "adventure," which reads as inspirational rather than constitutional; the substance — flourishing as a continuing journey, and the System's role as helping people find their next step rather than prescribing one path — is preserved in formal language that will still be legible in five centuries.

5. **Article II, Section 4 (The Collective) — new.**
   *Why:* Implements canonical principle 3. Placed in the Mission article, not the Founding Principles article, because it describes a structural mechanism of the mission (member flourishing and institutional capacity reinforcing one another) rather than a constraint on the exercise of power. The final sentence — flourishing of members and flourishing of those served are inseparable, never competing — forecloses a foreseeable future abuse: treating internal and external flourishing as a trade-off.

6. **Article II, Section 6 (Interpretation) — extended with the 500-Year Principle.**
   *Why:* Implements canonical principle 8, not as a rhetorical flourish but as an operative interpretive test: where an interpretation's long-term effect is uncertain, it must survive the question of whether it would still strengthen humanity five centuries hence. This converts a Founder review heuristic into a standing constitutional obligation binding all future interpreters, which is what gives it lasting force.

7. **Article III, Section 6 (Stewardship) — broadened from "Stewardship Across Generations."**
   *Why:* Implements canonical principle 2 fully. Draft 0.9 confined stewardship to governors' obligations toward future generations. Canonical principle 2 is broader: *every* person entrusted with responsibility holds it in trust for those they serve *and* future generations. The revised section states this as a general property of all authority in the System, with the original future-generations language preserved as its concluding sentence rather than discarded.

8. **Article III, Section 8 (Institutional Learning) — new.**
   *Why:* Implements canonical principle 4. Added at the end of Article III rather than inserted mid-sequence, to avoid renumbering Sections 1–7 and thereby disturbing every cross-reference built on Draft 0.9's numbering — a deliberate durability choice, not an oversight.

9. **Article IV, Section 1 — retitled "AI as Stewardship Technology" and revised to add flourishing.**
   *Why:* Implements canonical principle 6. The retitling makes the section directly traceable to the canonical principle of the same name. "Human flourishing" was added alongside "human judgment" and "human capability" so that Article IV's opening statement of purpose matches Article II's redefinition of the mission; leaving it unchanged would have created an internal inconsistency between what technology is said to serve and what the institution is said to exist for.

10. **Article IV, Section 2 — one sentence added.**
    *Why:* "An officer may use artificial intelligence to inform a decision; no officer may use it to escape responsibility for one" makes explicit and unmistakable the second half of canonical principle 6 ("AI never replaces constitutional human responsibility"). The surrounding text already implied this; the addition removes any interpretive gap for an officer looking to disclaim accountability by attributing a decision to an instrument.

11. **Article VI, Section 5 (Institutional Memory) — new.**
    *Why:* Anchors canonical principle 4 in the article governing the System's record, so that the duty to learn (Article III, Section 8) is paired with a duty to preserve and share what is learned. The closing sentence — "a lesson once learned and then lost through neglect is a failure of stewardship" — deliberately links this section back to Article III, Section 6, tying two canonical principles together rather than letting them operate as isolated provisions.

12. **Article VII, Section 3 (Entrenched Provisions) — updated list and corrected cross-references.**
    *Why:* Two changes, one substantive and one corrective. Substantively, the mission of human flourishing and the broadened stewardship principle were added to the entrenched list, since both are now foundational canonical commitments that later amendments should not be able to weaken. Correctively, the citation to "the primacy of the mission" was updated from Article II, Section 2 to Article II, Section 5 to reflect the renumbering caused by the insertion of new sections — an update required for internal consistency, not a substantive change in what was already protected.

**Provisions deliberately left unchanged:** Article I (Establishment), Article V (Governance), Article VIII (Continuity and Dissolution), and Article IX (Ratification) were reviewed against the 500-Year Principle and found already sound in their current form — each is technology-neutral, personality-neutral, and free of any assumption the new canonical principles would disturb. No language was added to these articles solely for the sake of visibly reflecting the new philosophy; doing so would have been repetition, not strengthening.
