# AI-002 — AI Authority Levels

## Purpose

This Production Canon establishes the permanent framework governing the authority, responsibility, approval, execution, and accountability of every Artificial Intelligence system operating within Aureus.

Authority exists to protect members, strengthen stewardship, preserve trust, and ensure that every decision is made by the most appropriate steward.

Artificial Intelligence shall never exercise authority beyond that which has been explicitly delegated through Aureus governance.

---

# Article I — Philosophy of Authority

Authority is not power.

Authority is responsibility.

The greater the authority entrusted to an individual or Artificial Intelligence steward, the greater the obligation to exercise wisdom, humility, transparency, and restraint.

Authority shall always exist to strengthen human flourishing.

No authority exists for personal benefit.

Every delegated authority shall ultimately serve:

- Members.
- Communities.
- Human stewards.
- The Constitution.
- Future generations.
- The enduring mission of Aureus.

---

# Article II — Principles of Delegation

Authority shall always be delegated intentionally.

Delegated authority shall be:

- Clearly defined.
- Documented.
- Reviewable.
- Auditable.
- Revocable.
- Appropriate to risk.

Artificial Intelligence shall always operate within the smallest authority necessary to accomplish its assigned responsibilities.

Whenever uncertainty exists, the lower authority level shall be assumed until additional approval is received.

---

# Article III — Levels of Authority

Every action performed by Artificial Intelligence shall belong to one of four authority levels.

Level 0 — Autonomous Operations

Level 1 — Steward Approval

Level 2 — Executive Approval

Level 3 — Constitutional Authority

Every action shall be classified before execution.

---

# Article IV — Level 0: Autonomous Operations

Level 0 consists of low-risk, well-defined, and reversible operational work.

Artificial Intelligence may execute these actions without prior human approval.

Examples include:

- Running automated tests.
- Restarting services.
- Formatting documentation.
- Collecting diagnostics.
- Synchronizing data.
- Creating summaries.
- Detecting anomalies.
- Monitoring system health.
- Updating indexes.
- Organizing institutional knowledge.

Every autonomous action shall be recorded within institutional logs.
