OAS-OPS-006 — Standard Operating Procedures Framework

Status: Canonical Draft v1.0
Authority: OAS-OPS-001 — Operations Charter
Related Documents: OAS-OPS-002 through OAS-OPS-005
Owner: Chief Operations Steward
Last Updated: July 13, 2026

---

Purpose

This document establishes the canonical framework for all Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs) within Aureus.

Every repeatable operational activity shall be documented using this framework to ensure consistency, quality, accountability, and continuous improvement across the institution.

---

Objectives

The SOP Framework exists to:

- Standardize recurring work.
- Improve operational reliability.
- Reduce unnecessary variation.
- Support human stewards and AI agents.
- Preserve institutional knowledge.
- Simplify onboarding.
- Improve quality and accountability.
- Enable continuous improvement.

---

Guiding Principles

Every SOP shall be:

- Clear
- Accurate
- Repeatable
- Verifiable
- Measurable
- Maintainable
- Accessible
- Version controlled

An SOP should enable a properly trained steward to perform the work consistently.

---

Scope

An SOP should be created whenever an activity:

- Is performed repeatedly.
- Has significant member impact.
- Involves multiple people or systems.
- Requires consistency.
- Contains legal, financial, security, or operational risk.
- Benefits from documentation.

---

Required SOP Structure

Every SOP shall include the following sections.

1. Document Information

- SOP Number
- Title
- Version
- Status
- Owner
- Effective Date
- Review Date

---

2. Purpose

Describe why this procedure exists.

---

3. Scope

Identify where the procedure applies and any exclusions.

---

4. Roles and Responsibilities

Define:

- Primary owner
- Supporting roles
- Required approvals
- AI responsibilities
- Human responsibilities

---

5. Prerequisites

List required:

- Training
- Permissions
- Systems
- Documents
- Resources

---

6. Inputs

Identify all information, materials, approvals, or resources required before the procedure begins.

---

7. Procedure

Document each step in logical order.

Each step should clearly identify:

- Responsible party
- Required action
- Expected outcome
- Decision points
- Verification requirements

---

8. Exceptions

Describe known exceptions and how they should be handled.

---

9. Escalation

Specify:

- Escalation triggers
- Escalation path
- Required documentation

---

10. Quality Verification

Define how successful completion is verified.

Verification may include:

- Checklists
- Peer review
- Automated validation
- Manager approval
- Member confirmation

---

11. Success Metrics

Define measurable indicators including, where appropriate:

- Completion time
- Accuracy
- Member satisfaction
- Error rate
- Compliance
- Rework frequency

---

12. Risks

Identify operational risks associated with the procedure.

For each risk include:

- Likelihood
- Impact
- Mitigation strategy

---

13. References

List related constitutional documents, policies, procedures, forms, systems, and standards.

---

14. Revision History

Maintain a chronological record of substantive changes.

---

Human and AI Collaboration

Each SOP shall clearly distinguish:

Human Responsibilities

Tasks requiring:

- Judgment
- Ethics
- Empathy
- Interpretation
- Relationship management
- Final approval

---

AI Responsibilities

Tasks suitable for automation, including:

- Data processing
- Scheduling
- Documentation
- Notifications
- Workflow routing
- Analytics
- Draft generation
- Routine verification

---

Documentation Standards

Every SOP shall use:

- Consistent terminology
- Plain language
- Sequential numbering
- Clearly labeled decision points
- Objective instructions
- Current references

Ambiguity should be minimized.

---

Ownership

Every SOP shall have a documented owner responsible for:

- Accuracy
- Maintenance
- Periodic review
- Improvement
- Version management

Ownership may be reassigned but shall never be undefined.

---

Review Requirements

Each SOP should be reviewed:

- On a recurring schedule
- Following major operational changes
- Following significant incidents
- Following repeated execution failures
- When opportunities for improvement are identified

---

Retirement

An SOP may be retired when:

- The process no longer exists.
- The process has been replaced.
- Constitutional authority changes.
- Institutional leadership formally approves retirement.

Retired SOPs shall remain archived for institutional history.

---

Continuous Improvement

Every execution of an SOP is an opportunity to improve it.

Recommended improvements should be documented, evaluated, and incorporated through the established governance process when appropriate.

---

Canonical Relationship

This framework governs the creation, maintenance, review, and retirement of every Standard Operating Procedure within Aureus.

No operational procedure shall supersede the Constitution, the Operations Charter, or the governing operational documents.
